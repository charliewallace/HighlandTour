PREFACE

Stories told to me by various relatives on both Mother's side of the family, and Dad's side were interesting to me as a young person, and sometimes boring as well. I didn't pay as much attention to them as I should have, and even when I did, time has dimmed the details. I wish I had taken notes, or better yet, I wish one of the aunts on each side had written her memories down, as I am doing now.

I realize that my memories of events are probably quite different from those of my sister, or brother, or cousins. The memories may have become distorted, after the passage of years and interference of other memories. I would like to be hypnotized and taken back to the days of my childhood, so I could relive some of those times, even though the experience might be unpleasant sometimes. Others have memories of our childhood days in the Matthews house that I can't recall at all. As time has gone by, I have added bits and pieces as thoughts came to me, so each book that I have passed out may have different page sequences. If I have made errors in names or places, or dates, I would greatly appreciate having them pointed out, so I can correct them.

 

For the McMasters Reunion, I have assembled a different book which leaves out my Mother's lineage, because this information would be irrelevant to those of you who are not descendants of Norman P. McMasters. I hope you will enjoy it.

The following is a poem I ran across that seems appropriate:

If you could see your ancestors all standing in a row

Would you be proud of them or not, or don't you really know?

Some strange discoveries are made climbing family trees,

And some of them, as you well know,

Will not your ego please.

If you could see your ancestors standing in a row,

There might be some of them perhaps you wouldn't care to know.

But there's another question which requires a different view.

If you were seen by ancestors

Would they be proud of you?

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

LIFE IN CANTON, NY FROM 1922 - 1940

HELEN'S STORY

by

Helen M. Wallace

 

Childhood was lived in a very different way, back in the nineteen twenties and thirties, than it is at this end of the century. There were no television sets, nor computers. Washing clothes was an all day chore, with clothes being hung out to dry on long clothes lines. No one worried if the clothes lines showed or not. A sparkling clean wash was something to be proud of. Clothes driers were still an idea lurking in the future. Instead, on rainy days, wooden clothes racks stood over hot air registers. Even the hot water was a problem, as often, it had to be heated on a stove. Our hot water heater was turned on only when needed, so we had to wait until it warmed up. We had a kerosene stove in the bathroom with a hot water kettle on it, so we could add hot water to the cold bath water to make a hot bath School and church played a very important part in the entertainment of children and families.

I have pleasant recollections of the early days of my life, and warm memories of my parents and older relatives which I want to share with my children and their children. So I'm starting this project, not knowing where it will lead.

DAD'S EARLY LIFE

I might as well start with my parents, Norman Pearl McMasters (Dad's middle name was in honor of a man named Mr. Pearl who loaned some money to my grandparents at one time) and Zona Alta Sprouse. Dad was born August 9th, 1892 and was brought up in the Plattsburgh, New York area. His father, and grandfathers were in the law business in varying degrees of importance. His maternal grandfather, Shepherd P. Bowen, was partner in the ownership of an iron mine and smelting business in the Lyon Mountain area. At one time, it was acclaimed as one of the most modern businesses of its kind in the state, and was very lucrative. They also manufactured horse shoe nails. He was known as a great philanthropist in his town of Saranac, N. Y. and was generous to the Presbyterian Church. He became State Senator, and served in that capacity during Abraham Lincoln's administration. He had other duties in the Plattsburgh area, one of which was involved with the prison at Danemora, New York. I have a portrait which was painted by one of the prisoners, and it is an excellent likeness. In 1868, he moved to Plattsburgh, to the old Lowell Mansion on McComb St. His paternal grandfather was Daniel McMasters, who was born in No. Hero, VT, in 1823. He graduated from the Univ. of Vermont in 1840 and came to Plattsburg the following year and studied law in the office of Wm. P. Haile. He was admitted to the bar in 1846, was superintendent of Schools, Justice of the Peace, County Judge and Surrogate, and U.S. commissioner, all at various stages of his life.

I never heard about my grandfather Charles McMasters' preparation for becoming a lawyer, but he worked in that capacity in the city of Plattsburgh, following in his father, Daniel's footsteps. Aunt Emily's obituary mentions that "he held many town offices, including that of Clinton County Coroner, Supervisor of the Town of Plattsburgh, and Justice of the Peace." I have a picture of him which is labeled on the back, "Father taken at Rouse's Point (on Lake Champlain across from Vermont) in 1917. Commissioner of Customs". My cousin Dan has written to me telling me about that part of Grandpa McMasters' life. He says "He worked there as a customs agent. Each day he drove in to Plattsburgh in his Model T, took the D&H train to Rouses Point, worked all day, then retraced the route in the evening. His health wasn't good, but he needed the money desperately. One evening at supper, he told us of checking out the car of some tourists (those were Prohibition days) but not too closely, much to their relief. One of the women thanked him and gushed, "I just have to compliment you, Inspector, on your beautiful teeth!" "Thank you Madam," said Grandpa gallantly. "Perhaps you'd like to see them closer". And he popped out his upper plate and held it up for inspection. "Charlie, you didn't!" wailed Grandma." Besides his law practice, my paternal grandfather was a farmer, growing hay, wheat, assorted garden foods, and apples. Dad lived the life of a country boy. He and his brothers and friends went camping in the near-by woods, learned to hunt and fish, played base ball, and helped around home. Picnicking was a favorite activity with the whole family. Some of the pictures I've seen taken during these outings show what a good time they had together. One of Dad's chores was churning the cream into butter, and as a result, his nickname was "Churn" He went to local schools, through 2nd year of high school. When he was in high school, there was a large fire in the Adirondack Mountains. He and some of his friends left school to help put it out, using shovels and courage mostly. This took days, and was very hard work. He didn't go back to school after that. He was fascinated with the rail road, and learned to send telegrams, and messages to help direct the trains. He attended the Delaware and Hudson Railroad Telegraph School in Whitehall, where one of Grandma McMasters' sisters lived - Aunt Suzie, and then served as a telegraph agent for the D & H Railroad from 1909 to 1912 in stations within the Champlain and Saratoga Divisions. From 1912 to 1915 he was associated with the Atlantic Coastline Railroad at St. Petersburgh, Florida, with service as a station agent. He often spoke of the good time he had living in Florida. He had fascinating souvenirs of that time. When he and Mother married, he was associated with the New York Central Railroad, working out of Watertown as a car distributor and telegrapher. So when he enlisted to serve in World War I, he was chosen to serve with the 62nd Railway Engineers as Chief Train Dispatcher, in France. Dan McMasters wrote me a wonderful letter about Dad during these early days, and of some of his experiences as a young boy. It's too good not to share, so I will include it here:

"In the 1920s, families still tended to live out their lives in the same valleys as their parents. Few left their native state; nobody crossed the great oceans. Traveling was done in easy stages. There was always a relative within a day's reach, and that meant 50 or 60 miles. My family traveled along an arc that began in Black River, near Lake Ontario, and swept north and east through Canton to Malone to Plattsburgh, with a jog south to the Saranac Lake area. At first we traveled by train or by primitive bus and later by car.

Our first stop was always Canton. This was fortunate for our Aunt Zona McMasters. When you travel with three youngsters, one of them is bound to get sick sometime, but usually only after three or four days on the road. In other words, we arrived in Canton healthy, and left the same way. We made one of these trips across the North country each year, until 1928 when my parents bought a place in Duane, a huge old farmhouse that they fixed up for taking tourists. In the Spring and Fall, we traveled North almost every weekend. Now we had our own transportation, at first an eccentric Overland truck and then a 1921 Studebaker Big Six touring car, the car I learned to drive on. This not only accommodated our family of five, plus sometimes a dog and a cat and a chum or two, but also coats of paint, groceries, assorted draperies and bedding. All cars then had running boards and this one could take a bedspring or mattress on the driver's side. It had side curtains against the rain (and also a hand operated windshield wiper). It was all of seven years old and terribly out of date, but it could do 60 miles an hour if the straightway was long enough. With a car like that and the roads of that time, you drove every minute.

But the trip across the North country that I remember most vividly took place in November of 1926 when my grandfather Charles McMasters died suddenly. My father was notified that afternoon and soon after left, taking Bowen and me. We traveled by train from Calcium to Canton, where Uncle Norman met us, driving his Essex sedan, which, like most cars of that day, was not heated. The night was cold and black with an occasional flurry of snow. Bowen and I huddled under some blankets. Somewhere around Lawrenceville we had a flat and there was no spare. Out we all got and set out down the highway, Uncle Norman rolling the tire like a hoop before him. He wore a coonskin coat, which was much in vogue at that time. There was a gas station about a half-mile distant, and the proprietor got up without too much urging and fixed the tire. On we went, mile after dark and weary mile, until we arrived at Green Lawns in Plattsburgh just before dawn.

Within hours the McMasters clan had assembled, and beds were being made up all over the house. Grandpa was brought in his casket to the front living room, as was the custom of the time, and there the funeral was held. It was memorable. Grandpa had a checkered past, but nobody in our family had more or better friends. The Clinton County Board of Supervisors was then in session and adjourned as a body to attend the funeral. The house was filled with mourners and still more stood out on the front porch. The procession of cars that left the family farm for the cemetery was a half-mile long.

A couple of days later Uncle Norman brought us all back to Canton, to the Evergreen. Meanwhile, Bowen had come down with a high fever, due to a vaccination he'd received just before leaving. That held us up a little, but I didn't mind. There was a huge record player -- ancestor of the juke box-- that fascinated me.

I should add here that on our trips to and from Duane, we always stopped in Canton, at least for gas and sometimes for service. Those were the days of high-pressure tires with demountable rims, the kind that separated the men from the boys. We needed all the help we could get, and Uncle Norman had it. If we got into Canton too late to stop by the garage, why, we stopped by the S & W diner for a cup of coffee and a 10 cent hamburger. Never ever after have I tasted better coffee.

Uncle Norman went to a telegraphy school in Whitehall . Later he qualified for a job as station master at Bloomingdale on the Adirondack line of the Delaware and Hudson Railroad. This was the heyday of American rails. Everything moved by train, and few were the areas in this country where you couldn't hear a locomotive whistle. It offered a heady life for a youngster still in his teens, and it had its perks. Once a barrel of oysters came in by Railway Express, intended for one of the local hotels. Uncle Norman liked oysters and so did a colleague at the station; they decided the oysters had been damaged in shipment and hence were undeliverable. All in all, he told me, he had worked for 14 railroads, ranging from the Canadian border to Southern Florida. He was what was called a "boomer" and had friends up and down the East Coast. Old-timers had them all over the country, and if they had a hankering to go trout fishing in Montana or see the Carnival in New Orleans, they put out the word and soon were on their way.

During the World War 1 he joined the Army Railway Telegraph Corps. I'm guessing at that title, but it was a distinct unit, not part of the Signal or Quartermaster Corps. His rank was Sergeant. Once he recalled one of his men finding a shipment of cases of what seemed to be fancy liqueurs. The labels were in French, of course. To the men's vast disgust, it turned out to be a hair tonic. He sent me several souvenirs including a German dress helmet -- black patent leather with a silver spike and a chin strap -- which was to have been worn when the Germans paraded into Paris.

After the war Uncle Norman went with the New York Central and worked in Watertown as chief dispatcher for a time, then was sent to Canton. The glory days were over for railroads by then. Uncle Norman read the signs and decided to strike out for himself. This was in the Roaring Twenties when there was nowhere to go but up. Every six months we learned of a new venture. One of his first steps had been to buy a garage and gas station, which became his base and which he kept until the early 1930s. When Hudson brought out the Essex to compete with Ford and Chervolet, Uncle Norman became the Canton agent. He had a radio repair service in the days of trickle chargers and morning glory loudspeakers. He had a man handling delivery in St. Lawrence County of Great Bend Bakery bread. He operated a Postal Telegraph agency in the office of his garage. He joined the VFW, became commander of the local post and revived the Canton Agricultural Fair as a fund raiser. And he built The Evergreen. I'm sure I've omitted three or four more ventures. After that great crash of 1929, the wheelings and dealings came to an end all over America, and eventually he was happy to find a place on the staff of State Senator Rhoda Fox Graves.

 

As to Green Lawns, the family homestead, it could best be viewed as Grandpa's Folly. As Uncle Norman said, Grandpa was the kind of farmer who kept six horses, one cow, and a flock of fifteen sheep, none of which had ever been shorn. He also had a flock of hens and on a cold winter day would fire up the wood stove in the hen house and sit in there in a rocking chair, smoking his pipe while the hens pecked and clucked around him. In his old age his income dwindled and debts mounted, and he would have lost the place if Aunt Emily had not taken it over for a restaurant -- then called a "tea room"-- and for tourist lodgings. She was the one who gave it the name of Green Lawns."

I am greatly indebted to my cousin Dan for contributing this well written document about my Father.

Dad had two older brothers, Clarence and Hector. His sister Emily was just a bit older than Dad was, and his sister Mary was the youngest and very beautiful. Theirs was a close, warm family, with lots of laughter. Their home was burned when Dad was nine (the lion picture which hangs over our fireplace was rescued from that home). I found a newspaper report of the fire among some family papers, dated Nov. 18, 1901 that says, "Charles McMasters Home Destroyed by Fire. Early Monday morning, the house and barns of Charles McMasters, about three miles west of Plattsburgh, were entirely destroyed by fire. Edmund Wilson, an employee of Mr. McMasters, kindled the fire in the kitchen stove about 5 a.m. and went to the barn to do the chores. When he returned he found the ceiling near the stove pipe ablaze and called the family, but the flames spread so rapidly it was impossible to extinguish them. So swift was the destruction that the family could not fully dress themselves. Very little was saved. The loss will be between $5000 and $6000, with $2300 insurance: $1000 on the house, $800 on the furniture and $500 on the barns and outbuildings." They moved just a little ways up the road and this house is the one I remember. We went there often while I was growing up.

GREEN LAWNS

My grandfather McMasters died when I was only 4, of a heart disorder - angina. Aunt Emily lived with Grandmother, and eventually converted their home into a restaurant, which I explain in more detail later on. She was great company for Grandma, helping to give the home an atmosphere of happiness and refinement. Grandma died in 1938, when I was 16. In those days, the dead person was "laid out" in the coffin in the living room, and I can remember this. I also remember the funeral. The minister was saying something at the graveside, and said "Our Father...". I thought he was starting to lead us into the Lord's Prayer, and I started reciting it out loud. I was half way through it before I realized that no one else was saying it. It was very embarrassing, and I started giggling and had a terrible time controlling myself. Grandpa was devoted to my Grandmother, (Clara Bowen) and was a warm, cheerful man. I remember sitting on his lap. There were some clay blocks that had arches, and columns and bricks, (red, green and dark tan) which we loved to play with. (In 1992, I went to see Eugene Fields' home, in St. Louis, with Wendy and her children, we saw some blocks, exactly like those, in a display of old toys. Memories of playing with them came flooding back. Eugene Fields was a famous poet who wrote mostly about children.)

The McMasters home was charming, rather colonial in design, and spread out. It must have been hard to keep it heated. There was a wide porch all across the front, but without a railing. In the entrance hall, hung a lovely cranberry glass lamp, and the stairs led up from there, to the bedroom, which I will describe after I've explained the down stairs. A short hall for hanging coats, with a narrow table for hats or mail, led to the living room beyond and there was also a door, immediately after entering on the right, which led into a "front room" which had a fireplace and lovely furniture. There might have been a grand piano in there. My grandmother played the piano, "The Church in the Wildwoods" being the only I recall. Interesting, and quite good, art work hung on the walls, one of which was of a child, done in a primitive style. This room led into a larger room, which was the living room. It had a bay window, and book shelves with interesting books, and objects d'art which fascinated me. There were lovely glass objects, and I wish I could remember them in detail so I could describe them. One was a satin glass peacock. A shelf had been installed near the ceiling where they had plates, and baskets, and interesting ornaments displayed. Grandpa's desk was in there too, at the left of the bathroom door. The pot bellied stove was against the opposite wall. It was a nice sunny room, and seemed to be well lit. Later, Aunt Emily had tables in there when it was part of the restaurant arrangement. Off this room was a small room which she called "The Pink Room". It had an oval table, set with pink accessories, like tiny pink hens sitting on a glass nest which had salt in them, and plates with pink flowers, and linens to match. In later life, this was Aunt Emily's bedroom. The bathroom was charming, with an old fashioned claw footed bath tub, wonderful smelling soap, corner shelves with powder boxes. Aunt Emily had a screen in there so when one was taking a bath, other family members could come in and privacy was allowed. She used screens very effectively in all the rooms.

Next, there were two doors. One led to a rather small dining room, with an attractive chandelier and an oval table. It had two windows, one on each of two walls, and side tables to help with the serving. There was a china cabinet of some sort in there, as well, for I remember a display of milk glass, and serving pieces for meals. This room was one step lower than the adjoining living room and kitchen. The other door in the living room led into the long, rather narrow, kitchen. Perhaps it seemed narrow because it was so long. There was a long table in the center of the room where food was prepared. Aunt Emily had an old fashioned wood stove, which had a water reservoir on one end. She used to make croquettes, using an iron flower shaped tool which had been dipped in batter. This was then lowered into hot fat for a moment, and would bubble, and splatter, until it was taken out and carefully removed from the iron, onto a piece of brown paper to cool. She used these to put creamed chicken, or fish over, or as dessert, with powdered sugar on them. We used to get the broken ones. There was a gas stove out in the store room , around the corner from the sink, which she used as well. There was a whole wall of shelves, where the dishes were kept, and a long low shelf under the table where she kept her pots and pans. Whenever we came to visit, we were put to work, washing and drying dishes, cleaning vegetables from the garden, or fetching things from the cellar. One time, in order to make pie crusts, she needed lard, and I was sent to the cellar to get some. I couldn't find it. I knew Aunt Emily needed it right away, and felt timid about going up to tell her I couldn't find it, but tell her I must. She said, impatiently, "Never mind, I'll get it," and rushed down the steep stairs, into the dimly lit, cool cellar, to a large barrel, which was half full of lard. I was looking for a 1 lb. box of it, on a shelf.

Near the sink, on the stove wall, was a door leading into a large pantry, where more dishes were stored. There was a set of Staffordshire dishes with a strange brown design, which had been used in my Grandmother's childhood home when they lived in the grand house on Macomb St. in Plattsburgh. Her ice box was in there too, full of lovely looking salads, and tasty left-overs. Later, she had a nice big refrigerator. On the opposite wall was the shelf where she kept her big cookie jar. She made wonderful shortbread cookies and some that were filled with fruit pastes. Aunt Emily sometimes made ice cream, using a hand cranked ice cream maker. It would be made out of fresh fruit from the orchard, usually peaches, or strawberries and nothing ever tasted better! We fought over who got to lick the beaters.

There was a door leading to a side verandah. Uncle Hector used to come into the kitchen through this door, carrying bags of groceries. By the door, was an oil painting of a dead duck, hanging by a string from a barn wall. There was an old fashioned telephone on the wall. It had a handle on the side which had to be cranked to get the operator to make a call. Everyone in the area used it, each person having their own sequence of rings. I remember listening in on other people's conversations, as I'm sure they did as well. Next to the phone was a door which led into a small office furnished with a large desk, a stove, book shelves, and in the corner was a low cubby hole where the blocks which I mentioned earlier, were stored. On the wall was a round, framed picture, a print of a horse head, painted by Rosa Bonheur, a famous French artist. This room had a tiny bedroom next to it, where I slept one summer when I went there to help Aunt Emily by waiting on tables. Grandma had a little English bull dog which I remember in this office more than anywhere else whom they called "Dixie". In those days, these little dogs were very popular. They had a number of them, and they were all named "Dixie".

There were other storage areas full of interesting "stuff" which we children used to paw through, and examine. Behind the house was a small cabin-type building which was used as the ice house at one time. It used to be full of large blocks of ice, with saw dust scattered around it, and on the floor to hold the cold in. Later it was converted to a sleeping room, and a few steps away was a small bathroom, which served that cabin, and another which was next to it. Some nights, during the summers, they were all full of tourists, so the dining rooms were full in the mornings and Aunt Emily was very busy. She had girls who lived near by who helped her, one of whom was named Hazel.

The upstairs where the bedrooms were was charming. The front bedroom was small, with a lovely old bed covered in white, and a wash stand near it which Aunt Emily kept filled with fresh water. There were often bouquets in pretty jars on the bedside tables and pretty white curtains at the windows. The next bedroom was large, with two double beds in it. This room had a dormer window, looking out on a lovely yard. A glass octagonal fish aquarium with a fountain in the middle, was surrounded by flowers, such as bachelors' buttons, cosmos, and Queen Anne's Lace. The apple orchard was in the adjoining field. On the wall was a picture of a little girl in bed and a large dog rested its head near her. I think the little girl was sick, because as I remember it, the dog looked worried. Death was not uncommon back then, so it played a large part in the art of the day, and in novels and songs.

The next bedroom was the one my Father had slept in as a boy. One of the drawers in his dresser had been chewed by a squirrel, and it always amused us to go and look at where the squirrel had done that, and imagine it happening, while he slept. Beside that room was a bathroom . The back stairs lead into this, and I was always nervous that someone would come up while I was going to the bathroom. One time, when we were there at the same time our Aunt Mary and her boys were there, The bathroom door was locked, and I hollered in to ask who was in there. Cousin John answered, and I asked what he was doing. He answered, in a strained voice "Shaving". I thought that was hilarious and it remained a sort of family joke.

The back bedroom was large, with at least three beds in it, of varying sizes. We often slept here. It had a sloping ceiling, with small windows between the floor and the end of the slope, which opened, letting in a lovely breeze. My Aunt Mary , who had married George Duryea, from Roslyn Heights, was there during the summer often, with her two boys,(my cousins John and Richard). They usually slept back there too, and we'd laugh and giggle long into the night, with many visits from one parent or another telling us to "be quiet and go to sleep". My cousins were from a small town near New York City and had New York accents. I thought they were wonderful. John had rather fragile health. Richard was younger, and very cute . They both had good senses of humor, and I connect our times together with lots of laughter. We used to walk through the back pasture to a small pine forest where we found Indian Pipes (a form of fungus) which grew up through the pine needles, and in the Spring, we found Jack in the Pulpits, Lady Slippers, and Trilliums. Sometimes we took a BB gun with us, and one time I shot a bird. The boys were always shooting, but I never had before. I was proud of my marksmanship, but regretted killing the poor thing. I never did it again.

One time, John and I acted in a terrible way I regret to this day. We found a box of carpet tacks, and threw them into the road that ran in front of the house. Then we sat in the lawn chairs near by and watched as a car came along and ran over them. Just a short distance down the road, the car stopped, and a man got out and walked around, looking at his flat tires. We thought it was funny at the time, but both confessed to feeling badly about having done it later. No one knew that we had done this awful thing, so we got away with it. I think we would have felt better if we had been punished.

Our Uncle Clarence and Aunt Grace often came with their three sons, Dan, Bowen, and Charles.

Dan spent many summers there between 1923 and 1927 working for Aunt Emily and during those summers, he says he slept in every room and out building at one time or another, and that he killed and plucked many chickens for her. She needed someone to turn the crank on the ice cream maker often, and he was often called upon. Bowen spent summers there helping too, and probably Charlie. We admired them, and saw them often as we were growing up. They often stopped to see us in Canton when they were going through. Uncle Clarence drove a Big Six Studebaker with a top which would fold back, and eising glass curtains on the sides to protect them from the wind and rain. I loved to go to their house. They had a small golf course in the field. They made it themselves with soup cans stuck in the ground to catch the balls. They also had tree houses, and "forts" to play in. In the field near by was a group of trees, and in this was an old airplane they played in. Neighbors saw it crash, and later two men walked out of the woods dressed in city clothes, and headed for town. It was assumed they were smuggling something. The controls were not damaged, so the boys had fun sitting in it, pretending to fly. I don't remember seeing this, but Dan has told me about it. When Dan was older and ready for college, he lived with us for awhile, and we all enjoyed that. He was like a big brother.

My Aunt Emily studied Hotel and Restaurant management at Rochester Mechanics Institute. She received a degree in Hotel and Restaurant Dining Room management and eventually converted their home into a Tea Room and rented rooms after Grandfather died. It was similar to today's Bed and Breakfast establishments which are so popular. It was quite elegant, and well run. The people in Plattsburgh came there for her beautifully prepared dinners and luncheons, and she served breakfast to the overnight guests. I used to help out when we went to visit them, and was taught how to serve from the right and take off from the left, and how to set a table correctly. At the side of the house, was a grove of pine trees, where she had wicker chairs and tables arranged, and usually a hammock or two hung between the trees. We children loved to play in the hammocks. There were out buildings too; a barn with hay in it where we played, a pen with chickens in it which she would have the hired man kill and prepare for the evening meals. Talk about fresh chicken!! After the head was chopped off, the chicken would flop around for a long time. Her garden supplied the kitchen with fresh lettuce, carrots, beets etc. which we children often picked and cleaned for her. Sometimes she was impatient, but considering the pressure she was under, it was understandable. She never made us feel unwelcome, (in fact, just the opposite.) I loved to go there. It must have been pretty special, because she often served wedding breakfasts, and society ladies had their bridge parties there. She called the place "Green Lawns, an unusual place to dine".

But most special, and also unusual, was Aunt Emily. She was the third child of Clara and Charles McMasters, a daughter coming after two sons. Clarence was the oldest, born July 20, 1883; then came Hector in 1886, followed by Emily. born.......... Dad was next, born on the 9th of August, 1892 and last was Aunt Mary. She was born ........ My earliest memories of Aunt Emily are of a busy, harried woman, with hair usually unruly. She had a merry laugh, and a cheerful smile, but was not what one would call pretty. Her eyes were her best feature, sparkling, and full of fun. When she was a child, she had been badly burned as the result of boiling water being spilled on her from the wood stove. Perhaps, as the result of this, she had psoriasis all over her body. It was a lifelong problem for her, trying to keep it under control. She used a special tar soap, and similar ointments, and one would see flakes of skin on the carpets from the lesions. I can imagine what a burden this must have been to her. It didn't effect her sweet disposition , as she always was loving to her family. She and Uncle Hector were very close. He did shopping for her, and probably advised her in business. He lived a short way up the Morrison Road, in Cadyville. It seemed as if family visits were very important to her, and we always had a great time. I remember a lot of laughter and talking.

Across the street from Green Lawns, lived the Gebo family, and behind their house the Saranac River flowed, where we fished sometimes. Aunt Emily was a good sport about cooking our fish for us. There were smaller streams nearby where we found polliwogs and craw dads. No wonder we loved going there.

When I was ten, I visited for a week or so, all by myself. I had never been away from home before. The first thing my Aunt Emily did was to take me to Plattsburgh to buy me a hat. She thought every well bred young lady should wear a hat when she goes out visiting, and she was going to take me to visit her friends. I thought the hat was horrible, but I wore it. We went to one house which was large, and had high ceilings. There were lots of interesting things in it, sitting on tables here and there, and nice pictures on the walls. My mother had always told me I shouldn't stare, so I sat there and rolled my eyes around the room, taking everything in, while Aunt Emily and her friend chatted. On the way home, Aunt Emily said I shouldn't roll my eyes at things, but should look directly at them.

She had a friend who lived in Morrisonville, a few miles down the road from Green Lawns, and we went there too. This lady had a little girl, named Jane, who was my age and she was invited to come to play with me. A few days later, she came, and we played in the attic over "The Lodge". This was a large building where Aunt Emily had spaces sectioned off with screens for people to sleep, with double beds, and dressers and wash stands. Some of them had cribs, for people who were traveling with babies. It was charming, and had a fresh smell of hay, because the room was adjacent to the barn. Anyway, to get back to my new friend, in this attic was an assortment of things which one finds in attics. One of them was a doll bed with springs which ran from the head of the bed to the foot - tight, thin coils of wire. We had lots of fun playing with this. I must have brought my doll with me. I remember a conversation we had involving Aunt Emily and my Grandmother. The little girl commented on the lady who talked funny. I told her that was my Grandmother, and she was embarrassed, but laughed anyway. Cousin Dan's comment about Grandma's "talking funny" was that she was born with a cleft palate, and that she was operated on to minimize the condition. But she was still left with a slight "honk".

I was there over the fourth of July. I knew that Dad would have bought a lot of fire works, as was his custom, and I waited for Uncle Hector to buy some. When he didn't show any inclination to do that, I asked him if I could have some. He went out and bought me a little package of one inchers. I had never lit any myself before. It didn't occur to him to do it for me, so I did it myself. It was raining, but it was exciting, doing it all by myself. I missed my family that night, and remembered the pin wheels, and Roman rockets we had every year. Uncle Hector didn't have any children, and didn't realize what I was missing.

 

 

 

 

UNCLE HECTOR AND GRANDMA IN CADYVILLE

My Uncle Hector lived in Cadyville, about five miles down the road, where he had a gas station with his living quarters upstairs. Grandmother was living there with him at the time, so I spent part of my time with them. It was wonderful there, because there was a pond right across the road full of huge frogs, and a raft to play on. Uncle Hector was a character, and had lots of friends who used to play cards with him in the evening. He had been a good base ball player when he was younger, so they talked baseball a lot. He was a short, round man, with a great personality. In the room behind the front office of the gas station, he had a table, and on the walls were pictures of dogs, playing cards. His friends were often there, talking about the events of the day, and laughing a lot. Uncle Hector had very firm ideas about politics, religion, people. He was upset about all the Canadians coming over to use the beaches at Lake Champlain and I remember long discussions about that. Racial problems aren't anything new.

Grandma was a dear woman, who loved us all very much. She and I made some cookies, and visited with the family next door who had a little girl who had caught her arm in the washing machine wringer and badly damaged it. I was fascinated by the scars and deformity this accident caused, and probably stared openly at them. We became friends and played together. The first night I was there, Grandma tucked me into my bed, and closed the door. During the night, I heard a terrible howling! I was petrified!. I thought sure I was hearing ghosts, so ran down the hall and climbed into bed with Grandma, who explained that what I was hearing were the hound dogs who lived next door. She said they often did that, and it was called baying. I had never even seen a hound before, much less heard one, and I lived in fear of them. They were kept in a fenced yard, and were probably used only for hunting.

Behind Uncle Hector's home was a sort of wooded pasture where huckleberries grew. They are similar to blueberries, only larger. Delicious! I picked pails full of them and helped Grandma make a pie. I can still remember Uncle Hector coming in with bags of groceries, and taking each item out and announcing what he had purchased. "Here's some jam, and I picked out some nice pork chops. They had fresh fish so I got some" etc.etc. My father used to do the same thing.

MOTHER'S GIRLHOOD

My Mother was born in Salem, Missouri. She lived there, and in Rollo, Mo. When she and her sisters were older, they lived in St. Louis, Missouri She was one of six sisters, whose names were Versie Thomas (because they had hoped for a boy), Ruth, Zona (Mother), Willis, Ida and Helen. Her Father was William Thomas Sprouse. He was ill with tuberculosis when Mother was a young woman and died at the age of 56. Mother never seemed able to remember much about him, probably because he was in sanitariums a lot, and not at home. He taught school for awhile, and did some farming I imagine. I'm quite sure he worked in a factory of some sort in St. Louis as well. I read a letter he wrote to Aunt Versie, saying how very proud he was of his beautiful daughters. Her Mother was named Nancy, and she was from Kentucky . They were rather poor, and sometimes ate possum and squirrel. Grandma raised chickens, and sold them. She had a loom and wove their rugs, using rags. When we were little, we had a lot of her hand woven rugs on our floors. They were well designed and colorful. At least two of them are still in existence. Wendy has one, and Connie another.

Mother told me a little about her childhood. She said when she was very little that she put a kitten in the oven, and it died. But when I asked Aunt Versie about it, she said, "No- that little kitty didn't die. We took it out right away and it was just fine." Apparently Mother's guilt made her think the worse. Another memory of her early childhood was about being with her grandma one time. They were out near the garden, and Mother had to go to the bathroom. Her grandma said to go ahead and go, there by the path where they were walking. Mother did her little b.m., and then some hens came along and started scratching in it, and pecking at it. Mother was horrified. Her Grandma just said, "Now Zona, you sweet hockey isn't going to hurt those chickens". She remembered that in one of their houses, there was a secret storage place under one of the stair steps. The horizontal part was hinged, and held tools, or things the family wished to hide. She also remembered that when Aunt Willis was a small girl, their grandma and grandpa Butler came over and said, "Nancy, you have all you can take care of here. We want to take Willis home with us." So they did, and Mother said when Willis came to visit them, the noise of all those girls used to irritate her, because she was an only child at home. Mother remembered stealing a whistle from the desk of her teacher. She said she felt so ashamed about it, she couldn't enjoy it, so she took it back and slipped it onto his desk when he wasn't looking. Her education went through the eighth grade, but during that time, she developed a great skill in reading, and loved poetry.

Mother worked at a big department store in St. Louis, Mo. called Famous and Barr's when she was older. One of her jobs was to model hats. She also worked in the book department. Mother told me one time that when she was helping people on a very busy day, she asked another girl, "Will you please help this woman?" and the woman Mother referred her to complained that "She was a lady, not a woman". Aunt Ida went to bookkeeping school and became a secretary. She worked at Scruggs Vandervort , eventually becoming the secretary for the president. Until she retired, Aunt Ida was head secretary for someone, her last job being secretary to the principal of Belmont High School in Los Angeles. When she retired, she and Uncle Homer moved to Enterprise, Alabama, where his people all lived. Aunt Versie went with them, but had her own little home.

All the girls went out to work as soon as they could, to help support their family. One of Mother's jobs was as a governess to some children whose family went to Lake Champlain (near Plattsburgh, where my Father lived) in the summertime. They rented a camp on Valcour Island. That's where my Mother and Father met - on the beach. (One time when Dad was telling us about his first meeting with Mother, I asked him if she dropped her handkerchief to get his attention. Smart Aleck that he was, he answered, "Hell no, she dropped her whole bathing suit!") Dad was working that summer as chauffeur for a family who was also there for the summer. They fell in love and he took her to meet his family, who thought she was delightful, I'm sure. But along came World War I, and Mother and Dad agreed they should wait until after the War to get married, which they did. On September 10th, 1919, they were married in St. Louis at my Mother's home, by Rev. Ernest Main, with her sisters in attendance. Mother's sister, Ruth, made her wedding gown (which I still have). Dad wore a dark green suit. He told me once that his wedding suit "scratched like the dickens". Mother and Dad moved to Watertown, New York, where they lived for a short time. Rouette was born there, and shortly after her birth, they moved to Canton, New York where Dad was chief dispatcher on the rail road . I came along on April 10, 1922, , in the hospital at Plattsburgh, N.Y. Mother stayed in the hospital for ten days, and then went to my Grandparents' house for a few days before my Dad came to get us and take us home. A picture of Mother, Grandma and Rouette was taken during that time, and I did a nice water color of it, which hangs in Rouette's home. At that time, we lived in a house on West Main Street, which is still there. My poor Mother was always very ill when she was pregnant, but she had three children anyway. My brother Philip, came along 20 months later, and he was the last.

I have a wonderful letter that Grandma Sprouse wrote to Mother and Dad after I was born that I will include here, written exactly as she wrote it. There is no intention to reveal spelling errors out of disrespect.

April 18, 1922

Dear Zona and Norman,

My dear children we rec the anouncement yesterday of the arival of Rouette's little sister. And it surely was a surprise. But I hope it is as pretty and sweet as Rouette. That will be sufficient. And that you may be bedewed with heavens richest blessings in rearing and providing for them. And finaly when the passing years shall bring that day of days that shall have arived for the birdlings to leave the parental nest that their love nest may be just over the way where their kidies can run over and see how Grandma and Grandpa is geting along evry day. There to dwell in peace happines and prosperity and never learn the true reality of that word Goodby.

But Zona write that middle name again as we are not sure as to what it is. And Zona you say the lump on your wrist has returned. And now since one opperations have failed I have an old remedy handed down from by gone years that was said to cure any unnatural groth on man or beast. And now I want to see it tried on that wrist of yours. It is as follows:

Four ingredience

Oil of Cedar Oil of casafras Oil of Hemlock, Equal parts.

Mix these three together. Then add turpentine enough to equal the other three combined. Shake well and rub freely. I have forgotten how often but I should think the more the better. So try it. It will certainly do no harm and might do a lot of good. Tell us in next letter how you got along this time. Were you so poorly this time as you was before? Well, we can see now where you and Norman had your dark days this past winter too. When he was laid up so long with his thumb and expecting company this spring too, but here is hoping it is smooth sailing from this on. I am just starting on a carpet for my front room. It is going all green and yellow woven double stran with a border. Well it is supper time so I must say good by.

With all kinds of loves and big bear hugs from

Mama and Grandma Sprouse

PS Zona, head your next letter with your house number. I have forgotten it.

Mother went to visit her sisters, when I was around five years old, and took Phil and me with her. My Grandmother had died by then. We went to Aunt Willis's house, and I have very dim memories of being there with cousins and aunts and uncles. My cousin Merle wore red boots, and rode on a pony. My Great Uncle Cliff showed us how he could fry an egg on a hot rock. We also went to St. Louis to visit Aunt Ida and Uncle Sylvan, and I can remember that they had rabbits in hutches behind their house. Uncle Sylvan was a doctor. But that's about all I can bring to mind. Rouette had already been there to visit, before I was born, so she stayed home with Dad, and other caretakers. We didn't get as well acquainted with Mother's family and home ground as we did with Dad's.

 

 

 

GOUVERNEUR STREET

Our childhood was wonderful. Mother and Dad were conscientious parents, who loved us and gave us every advantage they could. We moved to a house on Gouverneur Street around 1923, before Philip was born, and Mother asked Dad to cut a door in half, so she could watch us from the kitchen. In this room were our toys, and a slide. Dad cut the legs of a table to make it close to the floor, and sawed away the top to reveal a drawer full of sand where we could play with our spoons and probably little cars. Mother said that when I was born, she had to watch Rouette very carefully, because she was jealous and pinched and bit me every chance she got. Rouette was a very beautiful child, with lots of curly brown hair. She was adored by many and no doubt resented having to share her world. We lived on Gouverneur Street in a rented house. The hard times Grandma Sprouse was writing about in her letter were when Dad got blood poisoning in his thumb. His business on Riverside Drive in Canton was called McMasters Auto Service, and he vulcanized and retreaded tires, plus other auto repair work. I think he was one of the first to do these types of tire repairs in the North country. He said he got battery acid in a cut on his thumb, which became infected. All the rest of his life, he had a big round, scarred ball for his thumb, and his thumb nail was curved.

Up the street from our house a little way was a gas station which had an out-house behind it. In those days, they didn't have inside bathrooms for travelers. There was always a small wooden building, with a chair height board in it. There would be from one to three holes cut in it for toilet seats. Rouette and I used to go into this out-house to read the funny papers which were put there (probably to use as toilet paper). I couldn't read, and I doubt if Rouette could either, but she told me in a "reading sort of way" what the story was about. We used to stay in there too long sometimes, and I remember hearing footsteps approach, then the knock on the door which we ignored, than the receding steps, until finally, the owner of the station would come and pound on the door. He'd say "You girls come out of there!" and we would. I imagine our Mother was pretty angry with us about that, because he must have told her about it.

One night, my parents were out and had arranged for a friend of theirs to stay with us. I dreamed that a ghost came through the wall by my crib, or bed. I must have cried, and was comforted by the baby sitter, whose name was Grace Poole. I can remember standing in the crib and yelling. I must have been around two, so probably filled the crib up.

When I was born, we lived in a house on West Main Street, across the street from the Church family. Dwight was a very successful photographer. His wife's name was Helen, and she and my Mother became very close friends. They had two daughters, who were older than we were. When we moved to the Gouverneur St. house, it wasn't very far away from the Churches so they remained an important part of our lives. Mrs. Church used to take care of us when Mother needed time away from us, to run errands. They had a player piano which she let us play when we were there. I remember very clearly, sitting on the piano bench and playing "Carolina Moon", as I placed my hands over the keys as they were indented, pretending I was really playing. It was fascinating, watching the keys being pressed down as if a ghost were playing the song.

I have already told about my brother, Phil being born while we lived in this house. I was three months short of two years old when he was born, and Rouette was 22 months older than me. I can't remember anything about it, but he was born at home. Rouette and I were probably sent to the Church's or somewhere. Dr. Laidlaw took care of her. Phil was a beautiful big baby, and Dad must have been thrilled to have a son.

One Easter Sunday, Mother and Helen took us on a walk through Bullis's woods, where we found colored eggs among the bushes, and in hollow trees, or just along the path. We even saw a rabbit, and swore it was the Easter bunny. It was a very memorable experience, which I have cherished in my memory all these years. What lucky kids we were!! Later, when I was a young Mother, I tried to duplicate that day for my children, by hiding eggs among the trees in our front yard, which was 1/2 and acre. I'm sure there wasn't a rabbit to add the atmosphere.

Another thing that happened involving Helen Church was really funny. It was a hot day in the summer time, and Rouette and I were there playing while Helen was busy in another room. There was a horse fountain on the corner of Main and Gouverneur St., across the street from the Church home. It was kept full of water for thirsty horses, which were still used occasionally in those days around 1926. We decided it would be a great way to cool off, so we crossed the street, took all our clothes off, dropping them on the sidewalk, and climbed into the horse fountain. It was a little like a large birdbath, and deep enough for us to sit in. I can still remember the oval windows of the cars, with faces looking back at us as they passed by, on their way to Ogdensburg. In those days, one seldom saw naked children.

Mr. Chruch took many of the pictures of us during that time, I think, because some are of high quality. He probably took the one of us with the Chummy Roadster, and I know he took the one of me with my feet up in the air, at six months.

 

THE HARRINGTONS

There was a large hotel on the banks of the Grass River, which runs through Canton. It was built of red brick, and there was a row of leather chairs facing the window which looked out on Main Street. The guests often sat there, watching the comings and goings on the street. My parents were friends of the owner, and his wife, Mabel and Mike Harrington. They had three children. The oldest, Jean, was born the same day I was. Because of this, Jean and I were close friends. They lived in a suite of rooms on the first floor of the hotel. Mrs. Harrington was lovely, and I thought she was very glamorous. Jean would often invite me to stay all night with her, and sometimes we could pick out the room we wanted to sleep in. The chef in the kitchen became used to seeing us come in to beg for goodies, and always had something to please us among the desserts. A number of my early birthdays were celebrated there with Jean, and the cakes were made by the chef. They were beautiful, with many layers, and loops of frosting. I used to feel shy and a bit uncomfortable at these parties, because Jean's party dress was beautiful, with ruffles, and her shoes were always patent leather. My Mother made my dresses, which were quite plain by comparison. I wish I still had some of those little dresses that my Mother made for me. They probably had hand-made tatting on the collars, and I know they were beautifully made, because she was an excellent seamstress.

When Jean and I were playing one day, we walked by the American Theater and decided to go in and see the movie. I'm guessing that we were about 5 years old. We just walked in and sat down in the dark theater, unnoticed by the ticket taker. The movie was "On the Road to Mandalay" with Lawrence Tibbett. It was the first time I had ever been to the movies, and I remember the experience quite clearly. In one scene, a lion jumped toward the front of the stage, and I was sure it was coming right out at us!! And of course, the music was wonderful, with the famous opera singer, Lawrence Tibbett, singing "On the Road to Mandalay-ay, where the flying fishes play"! How exciting.

Well, when we came out of the theater, and were walking back to the hotel, the town policeman pulled up to the curb, and asked us where we had been. When we told him, he informed us that our parents were frantic, and that they had been looking all over town for us. This happened another time when we had been playing in the big touring car that they used to pick up guests at the railroad station. It had small seats which folded out to allow for extra seating, and was quite plush, I thought. It was in the big garage, next door to the hotel, and provided a great place to play house. We were so absorbed with our dolls, and little cars I suppose, that we didn't notice that the work day had come to an end and the garage was locked. We couldn't get out, nor could we get anyone to hear us. So we were locked in there for what seemed like a long time, until Mr. Harrington finally remembered seeing us go in there, and we were rescued. The policeman was told he could quit looking for us, as were found safe and sound. What a trial we must have been to them. There wasn't a whole lot to fear, in our safe little village where everyone knew everyone else, except the river, which ran right next to the hotel. Mrs. Harrington was very nervous about that river, and every time some child turned up missing, the grown ups always had to go down to the river bank and scan it for bodies.

Jean and I remained friends all through school, and beyond. We enjoyed our birthdays being on the same day. When I was planning my wedding, in April of 1947, she was engaged to be married to Bert MacKenzie from Canton. Bert was going to aviation maintenance school in Glendale ,Californa. Jean decided to ride out to California with my parents so we could be married together. However, because she planned to be married in the Catholic Church, and I planned to be married at my husband's home with Rev. Main officiating, she was married a day before I was, and I was her maid of honor. I married Clarence L. Wallace Jr. who had become acquainted with Bert while he was in Canton after being discharged from the Navy, in 1946. So we went to the City Hall together to get our marriage licenses, and had a lot of fun together, until Jean and Bert decided to go back to Canton, and take over the running of the Hotel Harrington. We really missed them, after they left.

Jean's story ends on a sad note. She had two children, and about six months after the birth of her second child, a boy, she had a sudden heart failure and died. She had dropped in at my brother and sister-in-law's house to give a gift to them for their son, Philip's birth. My sister, Rouette, was there too, and Rouette says that Jean was laughing about the card, which Bert had picked out. As she handed the card to Emma, she started to sit on a kitchen chair, but glanced off it and fell to the floor. What a terrible shock it must have been to Rouette and Emma. They called an ambulance who came quickly, but there was nothing that could be done to save her.

 

THE CAMPS

Dad had purchased land up near the north Branch of the Grass River, where he built a camp which played a large part in our growing up. He was an avid woodsman who loved to hunt and fish. This camp which he built was nice and warm for the cold winters, and had a round table on a pedestal where we ate. The pedestal was hollow, and we discussed one time, the idea of hiding things inside it, like silver and precious treasures. There was a porch which ran across the front of it. Mother taught us to play Flinch , and we use to sit out there and play in the evenings, by lamplight. I can't remember much else about it, because it burned to the ground when I was very young. Dad had loaned it to a Mr. Backus with whom he worked, on the railroad, and in starting the fire one morning, he threw gasoline on the wood in the stove, and it exploded, starting a fire he couldn't put out. Neither the man nor his wife were hurt, but I imagine they felt badly about burning down Dad's camp.

He built another one soon after, which I remember better, because we spent lots of time up there in the summers. It was in the same location, was painted dark green with white trim, and had a living room with an eating area at one end. The kitchen had a pump for water from a spring, and there was a nice big window which looked out over the river, and into the trees of the forest around it. There were stairs going up to a loft which had a railing made of white birch bark to protect us all from falling off. We could look down into the living room. There were nice big windows which opened for ventilation, but I still remember it being hot up there. We had an out house behind it, which the porcupines were always chewing on. We often spent a week or so at a time during the summer up there with Mother, and Dad would come up in the evening, after work, and for the week ends. To prepare for his arrival one day, Mother made a yellow cake, and I still remember how pleased he was.

The drive to camp was memorable. We used to go up the Pierpont Road, past Wallaces farm (little did I know, when we drove past, that one day, I would marry Clarence L. Wallace Jr.) with the maple grove and the rock pillars on each side of the drive with round balls perched on top. We passed Langdon's corners, the Roses, the country schoolhouse on the left, then the town of Pierpont, with the nice old houses, and a church on the left. Next, we were out of town, past farms and before long, the sand dunes near Colton on the left where Mother used to take us to play. This area was called High Flats. At this point, we bore to the right and went up a steep hill. It was dirt road at this point, but well maintained because there were still quite a few farms, usually weathered, with lilacs growing in the yards, and clothes lines with clothes blowing in the breeze. Dad knew just about everyone, and would wave to them, or stop for a minute and chat. A highlight of the trip was the General Store, at Cook's Corners, where we often stopped for groceries, or mantles for the Coleman lanterns and other things we might have forgotten to bring from home.. I always bought a Bit 'O Honey candy bar. There were all sorts of things in that store which we children were interested in. Not far after that, we would stop at a spring, which was on the left side of the road. Dad lifted a wooden cover, and there was a cup with a long handle to scoop out the water. We always got a drink there, all using the scoop. There were no germs in the woods, I guess . I think there was a trout in it sometimes, but maybe I'm thinking of my Uncle Clarence and Aunt Grace's spring, in Duane. Theirs did have a trout in it, for some reason.

We'd pass more camps, and then, on the left, was the home of the Martin Davis family, where Dad often stopped. I can't seem to remember much about them, but they played a large part in that country. They had a cow or two, so we bought our milk from them. There is an old house near Phil's present camp which had belonged to the Gleason family and was used during logging days. Some of the Gleason family lived there at one time. The Falls, up river from our camp, was called Gleason's Falls. When we were older, we slid on the large flat rocks there, and enjoyed the clear cold water.

In the Spring of 1928 or 29, after school one day, Dad took me with him to visit the Davis family at their home near the camp. It was warm, but I was still wearing my long white stockings. I had rolled them down around my ankles. I went into the house with Dad, and when we returned to the car, I hit my knee on the bottom of the front door, and cut it very deeply. I can clearly remember looking down at it and seeing the deep cut into yellow tissue, and watching it fill up with blood and spill over. I screamed, and they all came running with towels to wrap around it. Dad flew over the road to Canton, and took me to Dr. DePue's house on State Street to have it bandaged. He didn't take stitches (which I think he should have done) but taped it together, and bandaged it. I was terribly embarrassed about it, for some reason, and didn't want any of my friends in school to notice it. It left a bad scar. When it was beginning to heal, I fell when we were playing snap-the-whip at school and it broke open. My white stockings were badly bloodied, and I was mortified.

But to get back to the trip to the camp, the next point of interest was the old abandoned school house, which had a nice yard where the children used to play. There were some swings which we played on. Colorful flowers grew everywhere. Some were buttercups and red and yellow Indian Paint brushes bloomed in the Spring and summer. Mother packed a lunch sometimes, (hard-boiled egg sandwiches, or peanut butter and jelly) and we went there to play. I can vaguely remember being inside it, and writing on the black boards. Phil remembers a lady called "Red Wing" and a man called Charlie Walker who lived together in the old school house for a time. They had a child who probably loved living there. It was on the corner where we turned to the right to go through the woods, and over the iron bridge which crossed the North Branch of the Grasse River, and then immediately left onto the road through more deep woods to our camp. When we arrived, the unpacking would start, and everyone had to help carry things inside. But first, I think we all enjoyed the beautiful sounds of the woods- the soft, rippling sound of the river, and the breeze through the trees, which were pines, maple, ash. The birds flitted through the trees, as if greeting us. We could always hear Woodpeckers off in the forest pecking their holes in the trees. The smell was delicious. We could see insects of all sorts flying around. There were dragon flies, and mosquitoes and punkies which bit! Mother would put citronella on us to ward them off. The river was shallow, and the water was reddish brown, due to the iron in the soil. We could wade in the water, groping our way over the rocks and stones, and watch the bugs that looked like spiders, walking on the water.

Dad often took us on walks through the woods, and would point out vegetation and wildlife. We were intrigued with the fungus which grew on the trees, like a shelf. Some were quite large, and these we would remove, and take home to draw on. All the camps in the north country have these things with lists of names of people who were visiting on certain occasions, or of special deer which were bagged on certain days, by certain people. There was usually a beaver dam, and occasionally we'd see or hear a beaver slapping his tail against the water to warn his family, and other beavers that we were there.

One time, Phil wanted to go out with his BB gun, and Mother let him. He went down the little dirt road with his gun over his shoulder, and a short time later, came running back holding a small wood mouse by the tail, which he had shot!! He was so proud, and we were excited too, because it was good shooting to hit something so small. He went on to become an expert shot and woodsman. Phil loved the camp, and all that went with it. There was a special fishing hole where one could almost always catch a fish, and Dad wrote out an official deed of ownership to Phil ( he was about seven or eight years old at the time) for that fishing hole. He has it framed and hanging on the wall of the camp he has now.

My father had a terrible time keeping the road which led into the campsite passable, because during the winters, the ground froze and in Spring, when things thawed, the ground was a mess. He and Mother finally made the road out of logs which he lay side by side, tight together, which was called a corduroy road, for obvious reasons. It worked quite well, but required a lot of labor. Dad had lots of friends who fished and hunted with him, and they helped with chores like that, but my Mother was the one who always pitched in.

One of Mother's favorite camp activities was cleaning up small streams which poured into the river in the Springtime. She'd make little dams, and plant flowers along their banks, arranging pretty stones in places where they would show up the best. I loved to help her do this.

There was a man who had a camp not too far from ours whom we went to visit from time to time. His name was Skip Randall, and he lived in his camp year round at that time. He had pictures of naked women on his walls, and Mother didn't want us to go inside. He was good to us. He had a chipmunk which would come when he called it. To call it, he rubbed a cork against a glass whisky bottle. We spent a lot of time trying to do that, with only a little success. There were many animals up there which we saw often. Beaver were always building dams in the river, causing problems sometimes, and dad would have to break up their dams. We would see trees which they had felled, and we could see where they had used their teeth to cut the trees down, with wood chips lying all around, on the ground. Porcupines were all over the place and we saw them a lot, as well as chipmunks and squirrels. There were deer too, some with large racks of horns, and it was always very exciting when we saw them, especially in the spring when there were fawns with them. When my father, or one of his friends, shot one, I used to get very upset and cry. I remember Dad being mad at me one time, because he said it took the fun out of it for him. I soon got used to the idea though, as it was the way of the woods. Most of the men hoped to shoot one to supply meat for their families, as it was during the depression.

Lots of families were fed with food that was hunted or trapped by the fathers. Mother used to can the venison, or make mince meat out of it for pies. We always had a big venison roast to celebrate when Dad was lucky. I really never liked venison, because it has a lot of fat (called deer tallow) which would stick to the roof of my mouth. But that was because I dawdled with my food, allowing it to cool. I was always the last to finish eating. A big catch of trout was always cause to celebrate too. We had many dinners of fried trout, with potatoes and vegetables. The bones often caused a problem, getting stuck in our throats. Mother would have us eat bread to work them down, because they were tiny, fine little things.

A sad post script about the camps is that this wonderful camp burned to the ground too. There was a handsome young man from Canton named Paul Gilson ( I think that was his last name) who fell in love with one of the St. Lawrence students who was from a very wealthy family. They didn't approve of Paul, but the girl married him anyway, and it made headlines in all the New York papers. They came to our house to ask Dad if they could use our camp for their honeymoon, so no one would know where they were. Of course, he was glad to help them. Later that night, we were told that when they came near the camp they saw flames, and the camp was burning. Dad always doubted the story. It probably caught on fire like the first one did. Starting wood fires in those old stoves was tricky business. We were all broken hearted about losing our wonderful camp.

Years later, Dad and by now, grown up brother Phil built another camp up the river a little further. And for a few years, Mother and Dad leased a nice little camp just over the iron bridge. It was a great location, and they had it fixed up just the way they wanted it.

 

STATE STREET HOUSES

I don't remember moving to the State Street house. It was a double house, near the corner of Pearl Street, just around the corner from the Partlow's. Mother made friends with Alice Partlow, and I'm sure they were friends as couples. Mr. Partlow was named Angus, and he had one eye that was different, like a glass eye - sort of milky. They had two little girls, a little younger than Rouette and me, named Dot and Mary, and an older brother named Tommy. We played with them, and with the Gilson boys, whose parents were also friends of our parents. My father's gas station was on Riverside Drive, next door to Stanley Cornell's Service Station. The Cornells lived on the corner of State and Riverside Drive, and Mrs. Cornell ("Fana") was a good friend of Mother's too. We were in and out of these homes a lot, visiting, and playing with their children. Dad and Mr. Cornell were in stiff competition, so we used to hear a lot of stories about their disagreements. Mr. Cornell was really a character, and so was Dad. In spite of that, Mother and Fana remained good friends all their lives.

It doesn't seem as if we lived there very long, but I have a few memories of that period. There was an old man who used to sit in a chair in front of Dad's garage and gas station, smoking cigars, and talking to his cronies. He was there very often, so when he was absent for a period of time, I asked my Father where he was. He told me that he had died. When I asked him how he died, he told me that his heart had stopped beating. After that, I worried that mine would stop, and I'd die. When I went to bed, I lay there listening for the beat and imagined that I couldn't hear it. I'd run downstairs to ask Mother if it was beating. She'd assure me that it was, and then she'd tuck me into their bed, where I felt safer.

Dad had a small panel truck at that time, with "Watch For the Little Red Truck" printed on the side of it, to advertise either his vulcanizing business ( a method of repairing tires in those days) or his radio repair business. We went to the camp a number of times, using it to bring our stuff up. We'd be crowded back there, and loved it. On the way home, we could sleep in it.

I think it was while we lived there that Grandma and Aunt Emily came to visit, and brought us each a pillow which Grandma had made out of black oil cloth, cut in the shape of a cat's head. It was button hole stitched around the outside, and had the features embroidered on. I loved it!. They also gave us each new warm flannel night gowns they had made for us.

The next house we lived in was further up State Street, near Higgenbottom's store, where we could buy penny candy. The Catholic Church was kitty corner on Court Street, and across the street from the church lived Harry and Ola Bolton. Down State street from our house were the Hosley's who had bees. The Mulvaugh's lived next door. We lived in this house when we got a set of books called "The Book House". I remember the day they arrived. They came with a little wooden house to store them in, and it was beautifully made. We loved those books which Mother read to us often . Our Father took his turn at reading to us too.

It was when we lived in this house that we all had the Measles. Poor Mother had her hands full. Measles was a serious sickness in those days, requiring a lot of home nursing. She covered the windows in our bedrooms with dark cloth to protect our eyes. After we recovered, Phil announced to Mother that he was going out into the backyard to shoot our banty rooster. He had a little cork gun, which he used to do the deed. The cork never even touched the rooster, but the next morning, the poor little rooster was dead! It was sad, of course, but funny at the same time. No one ever figured out what had caused the death of that rooster Dad was a hunter, and always had guns around the house. I remember clearly watching him clean them, and can smell the oil he used to saturate the piece of cloth which he pulled through the barrel. He must have impressed on us that they were dangerous, because we never played with them, or touched them unless he was with us. He did let us shoot them when we were at camp and he was there with us to show us how. I suppose Phil was more tempted than Rouette or I, but I don't remember his fooling around with them, and he was taught all about them at a very early age.

One time, I was walking down State Street, chewing bubble gum and trying my best to blow bubbles. I met an older girl, and asked her if she knew how, and told her what a hard time I was having. She said, "Give me your gum, and I'll show you how." So out came my gum, and I gave it to her for a lesson. She flattened it out between her fingers, put it in the front of her mouth, and stuck her tongue into the front to make it bulge out, and blew a lovely bubble. Then she gave it back to me, and I went happily on, doing much better.

One Christmas, while we were living there, I received a scooter. It was gray, with an orange stripe and, although it was winter, the sidewalk was bare, and I took it outdoors to ride it. One day, Dad drove up to the house in a new car. It was a Studebaker, with blue glass flower vases between the front and back seats, a rope across the back of the front seat to hang a car robe over, and brown shades which we could pull down if the sun was shining in. It was brown, I think, and had mohair seats, which scratched my legs. We made many trips to Plattsburgh in that car, besides everywhere else. Mother used to worry that we might open the back doors and fall out, so she made a sort of cloth belt, with strong clips at each end which she attached to the inside handles, one on each side, so we couldn't open the doors.

It must have been about this time that Mother took us to a stream in the middle of a cow field to teach us to swim. It was near King Iron Bridge, on what was called, the "Five Mile Square". She tied a clothes line around each one of us, just under the arms, and would stand on the bank with the lines in her hands, while we splashed about, secure in the knowledge that she'd pull us to safety, if we got in too deep. After we became a little more confident in the water, she took us to a spot in the Little River where quite a few people went to swim. It was there that I learned to swim well enough that she felt it was safe for me to go alone. It was quite a ways outside of town, but we children used to walk there and swim with our friends. I was taking a short cut across a field one time, and stepped in a nest of grass snakes. I still remember, looking down to see what looked like a million little green things, swarming around my ankles!!

THE EVERGREEN

In 1926, when I was four years old, Dad purchased the land, on the East end of Main Street, at the intersection of the Peirpont Road and Route 11, across from the Evergreen Cemetery. He had rented a house in the country, near Eben, and we lived there that summer while he had the two story structure built. Living out in the country must have been pleasant for us. I have distant memories of taking baths in a large round galvanized tub, out on the back porch. There is a story about Rouette losing her temper, because she couldn't get in the front door. She thrust her hand through the glass, and was badly cut on her wrist. I suppose there was a frantic dash to town for medical assistance because she received many stitches, probably sewn by Dr. Williams who was our doctor at the time. Poor Mother, what a fright that must have been. Poor Rouette even more. It left a wide scar.

The Evergreen was to be Dad's business and our home combined. He sold gasoline, and batteries, automobile accessories, and Mother made sandwiches and served coffee and tea to hungry travelers. There was a room on the right as one faced the building, where he sold tools for travelers, oil, and some groceries. There was a bathroom off this room, and towards the back was the door which lead into a kitchen. Through that was a large room which had a nice hardwood floor. It was a tea room, with tables and chairs arranged around the walls, and sometimes there were college functions there. I remember a brown and red banner on the wall that said "St. Lawrence". St. Lawrence University is located in Canton. In the front of the building were two gasoline pumps. The ground was layered with cinders, which people used for ground cover in those days, because it was available from the iron mills. It was sharp to walk on barefooted. There was another building in the back where automobile repairs were done.

We lived upstairs. There was a nice large living room with a large stove for winter heat. We dressed by the stove, getting ready for school. I lived there when I started kindergarten. We had furniture which had spiral legs, made of oak, and at the end of the room was a day bed. My memories of the details are vague. The dining room was next, and behind that, a kitchen. At the other end of the upstairs, were the bedrooms. Aunt Ida and Uncle Sylvan came to visit us while we lived there. Uncle Tom and Aunt Versie came with them, in Uncle Sylvan's fancy automobile. It was a long, long trip from St. Louis. The night after their arrival, Aunt Ida sat in our bedroom and told Rouette and Phil and me about the time there was a cyclone in St. Louis, and they had to go into their basement until it was safe to come up. They brought gifts for us. I still have mine. It is a small orange disk with a face on it, and the eyes will stick out if it's tipped forward.

Although my Mother had been raised as a Baptist, and I think my father was a Presbytarian, they joined the Methodist Church and saw to it that we got our religious training in that Sunday School. The ten commandments were studied, and learned, and the Golden Rule was impressed upon us as one we should live by. I remember getting papers back with blue birds on it, meaning I had done well, I guess. On special occasions we sat in on the "grown up" church service. A favorite activity was counting the lights in the ceiling, and studying the stained glass windows. Mr. Carter was the first minister I remember. I don't think Dad enjoyed going to church, because I heard him say one time, "I think he's preaching right at me". This usually was after a night on the town, although I doubt very much of Mr. Carter had any idea of Dad's evening activities.

The church was a center of activities for us as we were growing up. In the winter, they provided hay rides, with carol singing during the Christmas season, and I remember several church dinners, held in the basement. There were parties there during our teen years, of which I rmember few details.

One time, as Dad was driving out of the front drive, Phil was "helping" by pushing the car. Dad didn't see him back there, and took off, with Phil hanging on the back bumper. He was dragged over the cinders for quite a few feet and badly scraped his knees. I can still see him in the bath tub, crying as Mother tried to clean out the small particles of cinders that were imbedded in his flesh. I felt so sorry for him.

Our favorite playground was in the cemetery across the street. It's a beautiful place, with large pine trees, and of course, grave markers with flowers on them. We sometimes took the flowers home to Mother. She made us return them. We had favorite markers. One of them looked like a small building with pillars, and a brass door. Chipmunks were everywhere watching us with their bright little eyes. I think Mother made lunches for us to have picnics over there. Mother was amused by my brother's way of asking for "a yittle yunce", and spoke of it often over the years. Freddy Roach's father was in charge of the grounds, and Freddy had a cart pulled by a dog, which he could ride in.

I have a clear memory of sitting at the kitchen table, trying to eat oatmeal Mother had prepared for me. It had lumps, and I couldn't get it down, so I went out onto a sort of back porch, and turned the bowl over, watching the glob of oatmeal turn over in the air, and then splat onto the black cinders below. Then I returned to the table and acted as if I had eaten it all.

Rouette and I played with our dolls one day, using a red wire rack which had been a display for crackers in the shop downstairs, to arrange them in. We had it lined with a blanket, and we sat them around the sides. At bedtime, we were reluctant to stop our play, so to make us feel better about leaving them, Mother made tiny little sandwiches for them. In the morning, there were only crumbs left, and we were convinced the dolls had really eaten them.

Some of my memories of the Evergreen are not so pleasant. Fana Cornell visited one day, with her son Junior. Sometimes, gypsies came to Canton with their funny carts, and people didn't trust them. It was said that they stole. The game we played that day, was "the gypsies are going to get you!" Junior was the gypsy, and we children were running around the living room to keep away from him. I tripped on something, and fell on a set of bare springs which had sharp metal pieces protruding from them. My lip was badly torn, and probably bled terribly. Again, Dr. Williams was called upon to sew up one of the McMasters kids. He didn't do a very good job, and all my life, I've had to live with the lump on my lip which remained after the stitches were removed.

Another job for Dr. Williams was to remove a nickel I swallowed. It got stuck in my throat, and he inserted a long thin rod with a grasping device on the end. He gave me the nickle, and I bought an orange-pineapple ice cream cone.

One day, Dad took Phil and me with him when he drove into town to go to the ten cents store. He parked across the street from the store, and told us to wait there for him. Phil decided he wanted to go with him, and went running across the street. I felt obliged to go get him, and in the process, was hit by a car. I was thrown into the air, and landed on my head. The next thing I remember was lying on the couch by the window in the dining room. Then, I can see myself lying on the back seat as I was being driven to the hospital in Ogdensburg. I must have had a concussion, because I threw up, but the damage couldn't have been too serious. Those are the only memories I have of the event. It was said that the man who hit me was a bootlegger, hurrying through town with his illicit booze.

Gypsy Lane was a narrow dirt road which ran beside our property, and crossed a railroad track about one hundred yards behind us. It was very sandy by the side of this dirt road, and we often played there. Sometimes, we pulled our sled with our toys on it, even though it was summer. Mother was very strict about our never going near the railroad tracks. One time, I had a terrible nightmare, that Phil was lying on his stomach on the sled, and it got up and ran with him towards the track, while a train was bearing down on him. I was trying to call to him, and couldn't make myself heard. What relief I felt when I woke to find it only a dream.

Rouette decided to buy herself a pair of shoes one time, while we lived there. She must have seen them in the window of Ellsworth's Shoe store, and went in and told them she wanted them. They assumed that Mother and Dad had sent her to get them, so gave them to her. She walked all the way home in her shiny new patent leather shoes. Well, it was decided that the shoes must be returned. Poor Rouette was terribly disappointed. When Christmas rolled around, they were waiting for her under the tree. That may have been the same Christmas when Santa Clause came and told Rouette that she'd have to promise to stop sucking her thumb if she wanted to get her gifts. She promised, and never sucked her thumb again, that I know of.

We children had the whooping cough while we lived there. Rouette got them first, and was the sickest, but Phil and I were given shots so we had lighter cases. We had to stay home, of course, even though we didn't feel very sick. One day we were roller skating on the wooden floor in the tea room, and started coughing from the exertion. We had been given lemonade shortly before, which came up as a result of the coughing. I clearly remember all three of us commenting that it tasted good, even coming up. After recovery and our return to school, I found that I understood arithmetic better than I had before I got sick. The rest from schoolwork must have been good for me right at that time.

Across the road and up a little ways was an old farmhouse, owned by Pearl Cook. She had a son named Richard who was a little older than we were. Her old brother lived with her and helped with the chores. They were kind to us and invited us to dinner sometimes. I was intrigued because her brother ate with his knife, even peas! Richard and my little brother had a battle with stones one time, and Richard hit Phil right in the middle of the forehead, drawing blood. It was a bad wound, and of course, Richard was looked upon with disdain for quite awhile.

The Thompson family lived on the same side of the road as the Evergreen. Their farm was separated from us only by a field. We often went there and watched them milk the cows. Sometimes we wore our bathing suits, and Mr. Thompson would squirt us with milk while we shrieked with laughter. They always had kittens in the barn, and lots of chickens, a pig pen with three or four pigs, a large meadow full of cows which were brought into the barn at night. We were allowed to help with chores sometimes, and often ate at their big table with them. They had two sons named Stanley and Warren, whom I mentioned earlier. After we had moved into town, their little sister was born. Rouette and Phil and I decided to walk out to their farm so see the new baby. It was late in the afternoon, in the winter, and by the time were to walk home, it was dark out. Our parents didn't know we had taken this hike, and worried about where we were, but Mr. Thompson must have called them, because I think Dad drove out to get us.

Although my Mother had been raised as a Baptist, and I think my father was a Presbytarian, they joined the Methodist Church and saw to it that we got our religious training in that Sunday School. The ten commandments were studied, and learned, and the Golden Rule was impressed upon us as one we should live by. I remember getting papers back with blue birds on it, meaning I had done well, I guess. On special occasions we sat in on the "grown up" church service. A favorite activity was counting the lights in the ceiling, and studying the stained glass windows. Mr. Carter was the first minister I remember. I don't think Dad enjoyed going to church, because I heard him say one time, "I think he's preaching right at me". This usually was after a night on the town, although I doubt very much of Mr. Carter had any idea of Dad's evening activities.

The church was a center of activities for us as we were growing up. In the winter, they provided hay rides, with carol singing during the Christmas season, and I remember several church dinners, held in the basement. There were parties there during our teen years, of which I rmember few details. On Thursday afternoons, when I was in grade school, every one went to the church of his choice for church school. There were some who didn't go, but most did. Our church was right across the street from the elementary school, so we didn't have far to walk. I remember a lot of talk about the missionaries in Africa.

THE MATTHEWS HOUSE

I don't know if business was unsatisfactory there, or why we moved again, but I was about seven or eight years old when we moved to the Matthews house on East Main Street.

The house was very different from our living quarters in the Evergreen. It had a small porch overlooking the sidewalk and the street. The living room was to the right as you walked in. The furniture was sort of mission style, with spiral legs and verticles. It wasn't very comfortable, but nice looking. The entry room was sort of a big hall. There was a large round register out of which came the heat in the winter. We could stand on it and our skirts would balloon out. It felt wonderful after a long walk home in the winter. In the back of the house, was the kitchen, and the dining room. There was a wood shed in the back of the house, off the kitchen, and a porch off that. Up stairs were three bedrooms. The bathroom was at the top of the stairs. Rouette and I shared a bedroom, Phil had a room opposite ours, with the bathroom in between. Mother and Dad had a nice bedroom with windows overlooking the street. She had an embroidered bedspread which I thought was beautiful, and her dresser had a lovely matching set of toilet articles, and lots of other pretty things. There was a little alcove off this room where Mother had her sewing machine. It was a great hiding place for hide and seek (a favorite game).

Lonny Brown and his wife, Myra lived next door on one side. Mrs. Brown was related to us on Dad's side of the family. Second cousins probably. An old man lived with them who was an uncle of Dad's. His name was Uncle Billy Bowen, and he was a civil war veteran. We used to go to visit him and he was always very kind and gentle with us. The Severances lived on the other side. June Severance was an only child. The day we moved in, Rouette and I held our dolls up to the window to show them to June, and she did the same. We loved living next to her. Her mother was rather eccentric, and one time, when Mother was smoking a cigarette, she blew the smoke out of her sewing room window. Mrs. Severance saw the smoke and called the fire department. It was embarrassing for Mother.

One time, the fire department had to come with a legitimate reason. Dad had come in with a box of groceries. He put the box on a small, wood burning stove that was near the back door in the kitchen. He didn't know that Mother had had a fire in it earlier. Apparently no one was home for awhile, because the box caught on fire, and smoke poured out of the house. I don't remember much about this, except we were all worried about our canary, "Ben". He was fine, and there wasn't much damage.

Dolly Banta lived across the street, and shortly after we moved there, Shirley Claus came to live in a house down the road a little ways. Her mother was charming and beautiful, and told us we could call her Betty. Up until that time, we had been taught to call our friends' parents by their last name, Mr. or Mrs. so and so, but pretty soon we were calling all these adults by their first names. Marian Smith lived around the corner, on Pleasant Street, and further down on Pleasant Street, on the corner of Judson Street, Ruth Currie lived with her Mother and two sisters, Margaret and Grace. We all walked to school together. It was a lovely time of my life. Our friends were there a lot to play with us, and Mother and Dad always made them welcome.

Genevieve Barber lived in another part of Canton, on University avenue, across from the St. Lawrence University campus. We went to her house a lot to play. We formed a club which met in the big room over their garage, and one of our favorite activities was putting on plays. Just across the street, there was an out door Greek Theatre, with a grass stage, and a semi-circle of straight pine trees for the background of the stage. We wrote our own plays and practiced diligently. In one, I was to play the part of a little girl, according to a diary entry. Another one was a musical, and each one of us performed and sang, in costume. Genevieve dressed in riding clothes and sang a song called "Pony Boy". June wore a sarong and sang "The Chant of the Jungle" which she learned by making our Victrola go slowly, so she could write the words down while the record played. We had a zither, which I pretended to play while I sang a song called "My Zither and Me". Our parents came for the final performance and sat on the grass to watch us. I think there were refreshments afterwards.

Our club met sometimes in the rooms above June Severence's Father's garage, just down the street from where we lived. One time, we found a bird's egg, and fried it in a tiny toy frying pan, on June's toy electric stove, which would really heat up. She had a tiny set of pots and pans. Because she was an only child, she had wonderful toys. Her father built lots of houses around Canton, especially out on East Main Street, where we lived. He had a lot of lumber stacked behind his garage, which we sometimes arranged into "houses", which we covered with blankets and played inside. It was wonderful rainy day play.

Mother and Dad were each very conscientious about writing to their family members, and insisted that we write to them as well. I came across a letter that I had written to Grandma McMasters when I was nine years old. It was written on childish stationary with a nursery rhyme on the top, "The Man in the Moon" and a cute picture to illustrate it. Here's the letter: "Monday

- Dear Grangma: The reason we dident come Suday was becase I had cut my knee badly. And the other reason was becase Charlie came to stay over Sunday. I am geting along fine in school and Philip is geting along just fine to. He is a whole lot better this year. His teacher is Miss Dode. Rouette is geting along fine too. her teacher is Miss Dingmon. And my teacher is miss Jennie Buckus. Mother has made a rock garden. It is just as sweet as it can be.

We have a little engore kitten just like our other one. It ketches a mouse evry morning and crys to bring it in. Philip has a little new pack basket. It just fits him. Daddy will let you know when we can come. Daddy has gone hunting and I hope he hast luck don't you?

I am sorry I haven't rote to you for so long. It is geting late now so I must close my letter. Please show this to Aunt Emlie and Uncle Hector.

Love

Miss Helen McMasters

P.S. I am giving you this paper becase I lick it. Dated October 26, 1931"

One of our favorite activities was making paper dolls out of funny paper characters. Jane Arden was one I liked, and I think Brenda Starr was being published then, and we used her figure. We would cut them out and paste them to cardboard, and then create fashions for them. The front porch was a good place to do this. We left our crayons out in the sun one time. When we returned, they were a mass of wax, all in a pool of color.

We played cowboys and Indians a lot, and Marian remembers that Mother tied our sleds in the trees in such a way that we could sit astride them and pretend they were horses. We spent hours doing that. I used to be Buck Rogers, and Shirley was alway Ken Maynard. Someone must have been Tim Mc Coy too. These were popular cowboy movie stars we used to be allowed to go see sometimes on Saturdays. We always sat in the second row, and chewed Joy Boy bubble gum.

Phil had friends in the neighborhood too, although with two older sisters, life wasn't always as he would have wanted it. We had an old fashioned tricycle which we played a lot with. Another favorite game was hop scotch, and sometimes we went to Shirley's house and played "kick the can", and "Mother, may I?" in her front yard. We were playing there one evening when Mother came to see Shirley's mother, and they were talking about the Lindburg baby kidnapping. It was on everybody's mind that summer. Mother was wearing a lovely summery dress of pastel flowers. The neckline was soft, and the skirt was loose and swirly. She had a hat on, and looked beautiful. I was very proud of her. My mother didn't dress up often.

Phil and a friend built a "club house" behind our house, and he was very upset one time when he found it had been tarred and feathered. It really was a mess, and Phil was in angry tears about it. I think Gibby Thomas was blamed, and Phil said "I wish my Uncle Charlie was here! He'd show him." Uncle Charlie was actually our cousin Charlie MacMasters. Another time, Phil had been out with Lauris Robinson, who was much older. They had been sticking straws into frogs mouths and blowing them up. When they returned home, they must have told Mother what they had been up too, and I distinctly remember Mother standing on the back porch, with her hands on her hips saying to Lauris, "How would you like it if someone stuck a straw down your throat and blew you up?" Lauris sort of shuffled around and said, "Oh, I wouldn't care."

With three children, and a house to run, Mother was often very busy. Mrs. Brown often came over to visit and observed that we children didn't do much to help her. We must have had chores to do, but I don't remember what they were. When Mrs. Brown commented, "Zony, I think you should get those children to help you!" Mother always replied, "They're only children for a short time. They should have a good time." And she did all she could to see that we did.

Our Father had a problem with alcohol. My first awareness of it was while we were living at the Matthews house. He was a gregarious person, and enjoyed the company of his men friends. He spent a lot of time in the bars around town, and when he had too much to drink to drive safely, the chief of police in Canton, Pierre Malterner, would call Mother and say, "Zona, I think you'd better come down to Crumps and get Norman. He's in no condition to drive." One time, it was dinner time, and Mother sent us to different houses in the neighborhood. I went to the Holcomb's house, across the street, but before we went our separate ways, we went into the closet off the dining room and prayed that Daddy would be all right. While I was at the Holcomb's, Mr. Holcomb teased me saying, "Look at Helen. Every time she bends her elbow, her mouth opens." It made me self-conscious. He was probably trying to take my mind off the reason I was there.

The depression had set in pretty well by the time we were living here, and Dad had to scramble to make a living to support us all. As Dan MacMasters' letter showed, my father was a go-getter, and always had something going. I think it was during this time that he hired some men to help cut wood off our camp property. He sold this, and ran his business on Riverside Drive, which was called McMasters Auto Shop. He became the Postal Telegraph operator and had his office there at the garage. But somehow, he landed a job in Albany as clerk to Senator Rhoda Fox Graves. He and Mother drove to Albany to interview for the job, and were gone over night. I remember sitting on the porch waiting for them to return, watching each car approach, thinking that next one would be theirs.

I think we were in bed by the time they arrived home, and they came bearing gifts. Each one of us got a light blue and yellow pencil with "George Washington Bicentennial - 1832-1932" on it,

and I got a little diary, called a Daily Calendar. Dad was a great diary writer, and kept diaries all his life. I asked him what I was supposed to write in it, and he answered, "Oh, just say what day it is, and how you feel, and if you are happy and what you did." So, here's an example of one of my entries, dated April 14th. "Today is Thursday. I felt well. I was happy. I went to school. We had lots of fun. We did long division." On March 21st, I wrote "I went to school. I felt well. Today was the first day of Spring. We play'd on the ski jump. Marian Smith was there. We had a nice time." One more entry is too good to leave out -- February 29th - I went to school. I felt well. I was happy. Daddy went to Albany again. I am going to sleep with Mother to night. This day won't be here for a long time, which is four years."

They really stressed attendance at our school, and if we were there every day for a month, we were rewarded with a half a day off. it was referred to as a half holiday. My diary entry of Oct. 28th reads "Today is Friday. We had our half hollow day. I did not have to go to school. Jen (Genevieve Barber) came up to our house. Shirley had to go to school. When Shirley came home, she stayed to our house till 9 o'clock". .On the cover and back of the book, I wrote my name in different ways, and in big letters "HOT-CHA!!!, and Mississippi over and over again. And stuck in among the pages, is a sheet of yellow paper, torn from an autograph book that had belonged to Marian Smith, dated 12/14/35, which says,

"Dear Marian and other names of affection.

I hope you never become the color of this page.

Your pal,

Helen McMasters"

During this time, I formed friendships which lasted all my life. Marian was one, and Ruth Currie was another. We were in the same class at school, and chummed around together, sharing secrets and giggling. We often did our home work together, and slept over at one another's houses. And of course we walked back and forth to school together. There were no school busses then, for kids who lived in town. In very bad weather, one of our parents drove us to school, but that was not often, because bad weather, especially during the Winter in Northern New York State, was not an unusual occurrence. There were very few two car families, and most of the fathers had already left for work by the time we left for school. Sometimes, when it was very cold, the snow would crunch under our feet. On cold days, we tied our scarves over our mouths, so we wouldn't breathe the intensely cold air into our lungs, and at night, our houses would crack loudly, with the expansion of the wood. Milk was usually left at the front door, and if it wasn't brought in immediately, the cream would be sticking up out of the bottle, because it had frozen. But we didn't know any different, and played in the snow, went sliding down hills on our Flexible Flyer sleds, and when we were older and had learned to skate, we spent every chance we had at the skating rink. When the temperature was way below zero, as it got at least once during the winter, our skate blades had to be exceptionally sharp because the ice was so hard. We tried skiing, but in those days, the only way to keep the skiis on was by sliding our shoes under a strap. The snow would pile up under our shoes and cause our feet to slide out sideways. It was very frustrating. And, of course, the only way to get back up the hill was to take our skiis off and trudge up, or try to herrinbone up. I never was successful at this.

The radio played a big part in our entertainment. Families would sit around it, and listen to "Jack Armstrong, The All American Boy", "Amos and Andy", "Little Orphan Annie", and some Indian show that used the expression "Ho Wa Ho Sawaka" for a greeting, and "Bo Ne No Ne Na" for goodbye. "The Whistler" was a scarey one we all loved too, and" Fibber McGee". Jack Benny was favorite comedian.

Christmas was wonderful in Canton. During the time off from school, we walked downtown, and loved the stores with the Christmas decorations, and the wonderful Christmas smell that was in the air. Dad always bought ribbon candy, and lots of nuts, which we had to crack. Mother put them in big bowls, and before long, there were nut shells all over the place. We couldn't spend much money on gifts, so practical things were often given, like kitchen knives, or stationery, shaving lotion, socks, and small toys. And of course, we often made things for each other. One year, Santa brought us a life sized doll, dressed in baby clothes that Mother had made for them. The next year, he brought a doll buggy for it. Another gift I got that I still have is a toy washing machine, with rubber wringers that really worked. Early one Christmas morning, while we were waiting for the house to warm up enough to go down to the tree, Dad climbed in bed with Rouette and me, and one of the slats that held the mattress up broke. We went crashing to the floor and laughed in surprise and just for the fun of it happening on Christmas morning. Our houses didn't have thermostats like we have now. The furnace often had to be started early in the morning, so the house would be warm for us when we were dressing for school. One year, we used wood for fuel, and Dad could be heard cursing in the basement, as he tried to get it going. Mother fussed at him a lot about his language, especially in front of us children. Phil picked up a lot of it, and on occasion had his mouth washed out with soap. I remember on one occasion, I hid behind the door and watched through the crack, feeling very sorry for him.

In January, there was usually a thaw, and the snow would turn to slush, deep in some places, that would soak our shoes. One time, walking home from school during a thaw, I found a fifty cent piece on the side walk. Another time, I found a bee, which I put on a piece of cardboard I had with me, and carried home. When I got there, I held it up to show it off, and it flew off and stung my neck.

The Maple trees which were all over town, would be tapped with small metal tubes with hooks on the top to hold a pail. Many people in the country had whole groves of Maple trees, and tapped their trees and sold the Maple Syrup they made. The Wallaces out on the Pierpont Road sold syrup all over the world. They had a big sugar house, and horses that pulled the sap in huge vats to the troughs where it was poured into big kettles that were over fires. This boiled until the syrup was the proper consistency, and then it was put into cans and sealed. Mother did this same procedure with our tree tappings, only on a much smaller scale, and we sometimes had a "sugar on snow" party, and invited our friends. We'd bring in dish pans of clean snow, and the hot syrup was poured over it. Because of the cold snow, it immediately hardened, and we could chew it.

Spring was always a wonderful relief! Even if snow were still on the ground, the air would be balmier, and the smell in the air would be different. Not long after that, Spring flowers would begin to show up, sometimes even with snow still in piles where the shadows lingered. May Day, which was celebrated on the first of May (as it still is,of course) was looked forward to with great relish. We made baskets out of folded over paper which we cut out, and embellished with paper doilies. These were filled with fresh wild flowers and sometimes small candies.. They were left on the door steps of friends. The custom was that if a boy brought you a basket, he got to chase you and give you a kiss. The only boy I ever remember bringing me a May basket was Arthur Williams. I was thrilled.

We played in a small woods on the far side of the golf course, where there was a swampy area. Cattails grew there, and we used the fluffy stuff from the cat tails to stuff doll pillows for our dolls. We had a favorite tree, a large pine tree, in which was a large nest. It must have been a hawk nest, but I remember it as an Eagle's nest. In the woods grew early Spring flowers, such as trilliums, lily of the valley, lady slippers. Along the railroad tracks, we found milkweed, which had green seed pods that lookes like parakeets. When they burst open, the fluffy seeds could be used for pillow stuffing too. These were the flowers we picked for our May baskets.

PLEASANT STREET

When I was about eleven years old, we moved to a house around the corner on Pleasant Street. It had been previously owned by Pauline Holcomb, when she was married to her first husband, Mr. Banta. Mother was thrilled to have our own home, and we were all very excited about moving. There was a big ice box in the kitchen, which did a wonderful job of keeping our food cool. Later, we bought a refrigerator, but in 1933, lots of people still used ice boxes. The ice was delivered by a horse drawn cart.

We already knew some of the neighbors, but soon were dropping in on the Robinsons who lived two doors up the street, and the Smiths, who lived beside them. Marian was their daughter, and I've already mentioned them. Mr. Smith was a professor at the New York State School of Agriculture. He had a wonderful sense of humor, and was a good carpenter. I used to like to visit with him in the basement workshop he had, where it was always cool. I ate many meals with them. Marian's older sister was Helen, who went to school in New York City, studying to be a dietician. She was a very nice girl, always kind to me. For my first formal dance, she gave me a lovely pearl evening purse, which I wish I still had. Marian's brother, was a student at Clarkson Tech. and lived in one of the fraternity houses in Potsdam, where the college was situated. He used to tease us, and I liked him a lot. Mrs. Smith was a fine musician. She played the piano beautifully, and so did Marian. I used to go to Marian's recitals at Mrs. Gaines's. Lots of my friends performed, so it was always fun to go to them. Mrs. Smith played the organ in the Methodist Church, and when I was old enough, I joined the choir. We practiced on Thursday evenings, at the church, or at the Smith's house.

I can't remember the name of the man who lived right next to us when we first moved there. His wife had died, and he was lonely, although not especially friendly. Later, Mr. and Mrs. Bowers and their little girl moved in. Mother and Fanny became great friends, and Mary was a charming child. Mother thought a great deal of Mary. Mr. Bowers was a judge. When he retired, Fanny used to complain about him being there all the time. I remember my mother scolding her about it one time, saying, "Fanny, that man has worked hard all his life, and now it's time for him to enjoy his home." Mother was good at putting people in their places in a nice way. On one occasion, a friend of mine was saying something derogatory about farmers, and Mother spoke up in their defense, saying, "what would we do without the farmers to provide us with fresh vegetables, milk, and eggs? It isn't right of you to make fun of them for any reason."

The Richardson family lived across the street on the corner of Pleasant and Cresent St. Mr. Richardson was a fine carpenter. They had two daughters, one of whom lived in New York city and had a little girl who used to come and visit sometimes. The one who lived at home was named

Doris, I think. When Mr. Richardson died, Dad ended up with his large tool box, which was filled with beautiful hand made wood working tools. We have two or three of them.

Our house was on a nice large lot, so there was room in the back for a garden. The garage was separate from the house. Later, Dad had the garage torn down, and another one which was larger, built that was attached to the house, so in the winter, we could walk from the car, up some stairs onto a porch and into the house. A family we knew, named Mousaw, lived next door, and next to that house was the railroad track. We soon grew accustomed to the trains going by and blowing their whistle. Often, we'd run down to the tracks and wave at the engineer, who was nice about waving back. Actually, the railroad tracks became a favorite place to play, and to walk along. Wild strawberries grew on the banks on either side of the tracks, which were a treat to have on our cereal. There were flowers we called "blue flags" , a form of wild iris.

A good short cut to go to school was to walk down the tracks to Main Street. Sometimes we walked from Pleasant street to Gypsy Lane, which took us past the Thompson's cow pasture. Our parents never worried about us doing this, as far as I know. We could tell when the train was coming and would get off and climb up on the bank beside it until it had passed by. Sometimes, we put pennies on the track, to have them flattened out.

Ruth Currie and her family lived at the end of Pleasant Street, and on the corner of Judson St. Her father had died when she was quite young. He had been from Oregon, so the Curries sometimes went to Oregon where they had relatives. Ruth's Mother was a charming lady, dignified, and always dressed nicely. Her hair was always nicely arranged . She had a peculiar way of sneezing. She said "Ah-hee", not Ah-choo like I did. Ruth's oldest sister, Grace, was a teacher at Canton High School. She taught physics, and was very well liked. She had a lovely personality, quite like her Mother, I thought. Margaret, Ruth's older sister, was blond, cute, and a lot of fun. She had lots of friends. It was fun to stay overnight there, and participate in their family life. They had a grand piano, with a pretty scarf sort of cover, and framed pictures on it. Ruth played paino very well, and it was pleasant to listen to her practice.

Humboldt , Kansas Nov. 20. 1859

Dear Brother Shepard,

It is some time since that I received your letter which I should have answered before, had I not learned that you had withdrawn your name for Representative. I am sorry of the necessity for such withdrawal for I fear it will injure your future prospects for political advancement. We have so many elections here and so much political excitement that I get sick and tired of it. My name is before the people again for the office of district clerk. I have for my opponent Mr. Clark, the same person that run in opposition to me for Delegate. I think I shall beat him easier than before but can't tell. I have had the extreme pleasure of making some enemies of my former friends from the course I saw fit to pursue in the late canvass. I have succeeded thus far, politically but there is a chance for failure. I now think some of paying you a visit this winter and shall do so unless I change my mind or something happens to prevent. Money matters are very tight and it is extremely hard to get hold of any for services which is all I have now to depend upon for a living and that of a different kind than what I used to perform. I have never regretted coming to Kansas, although I do not doubt but for the same length of time, I could have earned more money to have remained East. I have learned something of the world and the rest of mankind since I have been here. I have many friends and some enemies of course. The kind of life I had here is in some respects pleasant. It is rather more independent than being behind a counter slaving for old Villas.(He is referring to Samuel Flint Vilas who had a wholesale business in Plattsburgh. He sold dry goods, and Yankee notions, and also manufactured and sold tinware.) I think my general health much better than when I left although I have had considerable sickness. Out of doors life agrees with me pretty well. I have had no ague for a couple of months and do not expect to have this winter. We are having warm dry weather for this season of the year. We have had but one snow squall and less than a week of cold weather. The coldest the mercury stood at 11 degrees. I am pleased to learn that your business has been so successful and that you are satisfied with it. I hope that your connection with Kingslands will be a continued success.

I am also pleased to learn that there is a chance for me to succeed in inducing Mary to return with me should I go East this winter. My heart remains untouched as yet. By that I don't mean that I never was in love for you know that I fall in love with all school marms and some that are not, But matrimonial love is what I refer to. I have to regret the absence of school marms here and lead a very dull life as far as ladies society is concerned and spend my idle hours in company with others who are as unfortunate as myself. I have just left a bachelor retreat where 'its said the Signor clique congregate to plot against the wishes of certain political aspirants. I feel perfectly at home in a well regulated bachelors' abode where I can smoke and tell stories until I am surfeited with vulgarity. You must speak a good word for me among the ladies so I can have a fair chance when I return. Remember me to inquiring friends,

Kiss the babies for me and do not let them forget Uncle Jim. I should be pleased to have a chance to muss them around a little. With much love to all - as Ever, your J. H. Signor

Another interesting family background is our connection to Roger Williams, which I'll stick in here . The ancestry of Roger Williams back to Morgan Williams, who married a sister of Thomas Cromwell; his eldest son, Richard, was knighted by Henry VIII who requested him to assume the name of Cromwell, Earl of Essex, with whom he was connected. He was sheriff of Cambridge, and Huntingdon in 1541, M.P. for Huntingdon in 1542. He married in 1518 Frances, daughter of and co-heiress of Sir Thomas Murfyn, of Ely, Lord Mayor of London. His son, Henry, was knighted by Queen Elizabeth, in 1563 and was M.P. for Huntingdon. He married Joan, daughter of Sir Ralph Warren. One of the grandsons, Oliver, was the Protector. A daughter of Henry married a Pemberton, probably Roger, a grandson of Sir John Pemberton, of Tanhope, and they were the parents of Alice who married James Williams and were the parents of Roger.

Roger was born sometime between 1599 and 1603, and entered as a scholar at the Charter House School in London, on June 16, 1621 and at Pembroke College, Cambridge University, graduating in 1624. He studied law with Sir Edward Coke. In 1629 was Chaplain to Sir William Masham of Oles Co., Essex. Driven out of England by Archbishop Laud of the English Church, he took passage to New England in the Lyon, of Bristol, with his new bride, Mary Barnard (married December 1629). After a long stormy voyage, they dropped anchor near Boston harbor on February 5, 1631. Roger Williams' strong beliefs about the separation of of church and state, and and his feelings that people should be allowed to choose their religion caused him to clash with John Cotton and the Bay Colony elders. Even worse, Williams preached that the Indians should be paid for their land. On October 9, 1635 the court found him guilty of preaching "newe and dangerous ideas" and sentenced him to leave Massachusetts within six weeks. He fled on foot into the wilderness. with the help of the Narragansett Indians, he survived the winter, and eventually ended up in the area which is now Providence, Rhode Island. There are numerous books about Roger Williams, some of which are available through Amazon.com., and in your local library. ROGER WILLIAMS

Purpose

Roger Williams was one of the first men in modern government to advocate complete religious freedom. He played an important part in the designing of the government of Rhode Island, which was based on the consent of the settlers, and which provided for frequent elections, a flexible constitution, local home rule and separation of church and state. This government became an important part of the American democratic tradition which was later expressed in the U. S, Constitution

In this paper, I hope to show that Roger Williams was the first man to possess the characteristics generally associated with Americans today, such as humanitarianism, and belief in religious freedom, liberty of conscience, civil right, and the democratic process. In other words, I wish to show that Roger Williams was the first "American".

No one is quite sure of the year when Roger Williams was born. A biography written in 1834 said he was born in Wales in 1599, and was a relative of Oliver Cromwell. Another writer places his birthdate in Cornwall, 1603. Still other writers record dates of 1604, 1605, and even 1607.

However, the most accredited version today says he was born around 1603, son of a merchant tailor named James Williams, and his wife Alice, in London. In support of this, Alice William's will (1634 leaves "10 pounds yearly for 20 years to be paid to my son, Roger Williams, now beyond the seas".

Records of the Charter House, London, Show that Roger Williams was admitted in 1621, with the support of Sir Edward Coke, a friend. He gained admittance to Pembroke College at Cambridge University later on, and obtained his B.A. degree there in 1626-1627.

He spent a few years after his graduation as a chaplain in the home of Sir William Masham. He fell in love with a cousin of Lady Masham, but was unable to obtain her hand, because he didn't have enough money.

Disappointed, he left the chaplaincy and began to preach against the persecution of religious dissenters that Archbishop Land was carrying on at the time. This, of course, duly drew the Archbishop's attention. Roger wrote, "Truly, it was as bitter as death tome when Bishop Land persued me out of this land."

On December 12, 1630, he took passage to New England on the Lyon, of Bristol, with his new bride Mary (Who is somewhat of a mystery). After a long stormy voyage, they arrived in Boston, on February 5, 1631.

JOHN H. BUTLER

An interesting story about my great Grandfather, John H. Butler follows, which took place in the Senate session, 1902, House Bill No. 297. Tues. March 11, 1902 -'The following bill, which originated in the House of Representatives, was read for the first time and ordered printed, Viz.: -- An act for the benefit of John H. Butler, of Allen county. Whereas, upon the claim of John H. Butler, that in 1865 and 1866, he acted as sheriff of Allen county, Kentucky, and that while he had in his possession the sum of five thousands seven hundred dollars of taxes and revenues belonging to the State, he was, on January 27, 1867, assaulted by a band of robbers and robbed of all the said money, and that afterwards he and his sureties paid the whole of said amount into the State Treasury; now, therefore, be if enacted by the General Assemble of the commonwealth of Kentucky: That John H. Butler is hereby authorized to bring suit against the Commonwealth of Kentucky in the Franklin circuit court for the recovery of five thousand seven hundred dollars, or any part thereof to which he may be legally entitled: Provided, Nor interest shall be recovered on said sum or any part thereof.

"A PETITION TO THE KENTUCKY LEGISLATURE"

We the undersigned citizens of Allen and Warren counties, Kentucky, personally acquainted with John H. Butler and have known him for many years and knew him when he was sheriff of Allen county, Ky., during the years of 1865, 1866. We know of the circumstances of his having been robbed while on his way to Bowling Green from Allen County with several thousand dollars to deposit in the banks - there being no bank or place of safely keeping in Scottsville - the State and county taxes. The sheriff was compelled to travel through the county to Bowling Green, it being the nearest point for depositing. It was upon one occasion of this kind that he was robbed, as has since been confessed by at least one of the highwaymen. Evidence of said confession and amount taken is herewith submitted. Therefore, all the premises considered, we would earnestly request and petition the General Assembly of the State of Kentucky to consider the claim of said Butler, and that an appropriation be made, or other such legislation be made, as is considered proper reimbursing him for the said loss, together with his securities who were on his bond at the time."

There are twenty-eight signatures of men ranging from sheriffs, Justice of the Peace, School superintendent, ex-senators, a carpenter etc. to testify to the truth of this document. It is finished with "I have no personal knowledge of the matter set fourth in the foregoing petition, but if the facts stated in regard to same are true- which I cannot doubt from the signatures to said petition- I am of the opinion that Mr. Butler is entitled to the relief asked. And it's signed by Judge Eighth Judicial District of Kentucky, W.E. Settle.

This is followed by five statements from different people stating that the robber, Elias Woodall, confessed to the robbery and expressed regret. He described the way the money was wrapped, and how John Butler acted. Then it's followed by a statement from John H. Butler, as follows:

"To the Honorable Members of the General Assembly of the common-wealth of Kentucky Assembled: "Dear Sirs: In as brief a manner as possible, I desire to impress upon your mind, as representatives of the Commonwealth of Kentucky, the circumstances and nature of my claim vs. the State of Kentucky, for the reimbursement, for the loss of money belonging to said State; Which, while acting as sheriff of Allen County, Kentucky, on January 27th, 1867, while in the line of duty and acting upon my best judgment and doing what I could and all that I knew to get the money ($5700) five-thousand and seven hundred dollars, to the bank at Bowling Green, Kentucky, for the purpose of placing same to the credit of said State of Kentucky. I was upon this occasion overtaken upon the highway by three unknown robbers and robbed of said amount, all of which, myself and securities soon afterwards made good to the State by paying the whole of said amount to the State Treasurer.

It will be remembered that in those perilous days of reconstruction, after the close of the Civil War, that collectors as well as others, having charge of money, were not only in great danger of losing same; but also the loss of life. Be it also remembered at the time of this unfortunate circumstance occurred, there was no bank or other safe place of deposit kept in said county of Allen, therefore, there was no other possible way or means of getting the revenue to the bank, except to carry the same overland in currency. I had at the time the robbing occurred, in my possession besides the amount taken, about $1700 in checks, which the robbers did not take.

Now then, the question might occur as to why this claim has not been settled or disposed of before this time. I will give the reasons briefly, as they exist: First-immediately after the robbing occurred, (the assembly being in session at the time) there was a strong petition produced and sent up by Attorneys G.M. Mulligan and David Walker and a bill was offered to have the amount remitted. Hon. John J. Gatewood was the Rep from Allen County at the time and prepared my Bill, and Hon. B. W. Stone of Barren Co. was in the Senate from our District, and also agreed to favor the Bill; it passed the lower house by a respectable majority, but before it reached the Senate, some person to me unknown, sent a postal card addressed to the General Assembly (without any name signed to it) asking that if Sheriff Butler's Bill had not already been passed, to hold same up and not pass it; that there was being strong suspicion that the whole thing was a fraud. So on the day the bill was expected to be called up in the Senate and with the card before it (Dr. B W Stone being absent) the bill was lost by about two votes. Then Mr. Gatewood introduced another Bill asking that an extension of time, a limit of one year be given to me to sell off my property and finish collecting and pay in said amount.

That Bill was passed provided that the securities would stand on said bond-all of which agreed but one-consequently I was sued at once and my property sold at a very great sacrifice; thus ending the matter at the time. So the case rested until satisfactory proof of the facts became developed as well as the death confession of one of the robbers, when the case was again agitated and another petition procured and sent up to Dr. Robert Walker, of Scottsville, Allen county, Kentucky, who was in the Senate about the year 1887; and after he had received all the necessary papers in the case he lost or misplaced said papers. He then wrote me what had happened and for me to come to Frankfort at once; that he was of the opinion that if I was present before the committee, with ;my statements and his, that such proofs did exist, then the Bill could perhaps be passed. I responded at once and when we went before the committee, who was of the opinion that the Bill should not be reported without the real testimony, but that if I would go back down to Allen county and retake the proof, as stated, they would then report same and try to pass the bill. I did so; this was already late in the session and by the time it took me to perform the task the members had many become absent, and the chairman had gone home to the bedside of his sick wife. So there rested the matter until the last meeting of the Assembly, when a relief bill was passed for me to bring an action vs. the State for the recovery of the principle of said amount, providing no interest should be recovered. Said bill was passed about four days before the session closed and did not reach the Governor, for his approval, until after said Assembly had adjourned; and he vetoed same for reasons which you are herewith respectfully referred.

Now Gentlemen; It is not my will or desire to be tedious in presenting this matter. There are many minor facts connected with the case, but we will let the matter rest at this, and submit a part of the testimony as part hereof. I desire to say in the conclusion, that so far as my knowledge extends, it has ever been a precedent practiced no only in the sovereign old State of Kentucky, but also all the states of the Union, to properly reinstate public servants who performed their duty faithfully and in good faith, in trying to deliver the same. After a careful and conscientious consideration of the facts in this case, I would most earnestly solicit your undivided support in my behalf, in final prosecution of my claim at this session.

And I shall ever remain, Yours Truly,

John H. Butler"
